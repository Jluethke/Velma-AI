# Parenting Discipline Playbook

Helps you set boundaries, enforce consequences, and handle meltdowns without losing your own mind (or your temper). This isn't about punishment -- it's about teaching kids what's expected and what happens when they push past the line. Covers toddlers through teens, because what works on a 3-year-old will backfire spectacularly on a 13-year-old. Built for parents who are tired of yelling and want something that actually works without guilt or shame.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: ASSESS      --> Understand the child's age, temperament, and what's actually going on
PHASE 2: FRAMEWORK   --> Build age-appropriate boundaries and consequence systems
PHASE 3: SCRIPTS     --> Create word-for-word responses for the hardest moments
PHASE 4: DE-ESCALATE --> Learn to regulate yourself before regulating your kid
PHASE 5: REPAIR      --> Fix ruptures after things go sideways (because they will)
```

## Inputs
- child_profile: object -- Age, temperament (easygoing, strong-willed, anxious, sensory-sensitive), any diagnoses (ADHD, autism, etc.), and what they're like on their best and worst days
- current_challenges: array -- The specific behaviors driving you up the wall: tantrums, backtalk, hitting, refusing homework, screen time battles, sibling fights, lying, defiance
- household_context: object -- Single parent or two-parent, co-parenting situation, other kids in the house, any major stressors (move, divorce, new baby, job loss)
- discipline_history: object -- (Optional) What you've tried before, what worked temporarily, what made things worse, your own upbringing and what you want to do differently
- goals: object -- (Optional) What "good discipline" looks like to you: cooperation, respect, independence, emotional regulation, or just surviving bedtime without a battle

## Outputs
- behavior_assessment: object -- What's driving the behavior (attention, control, overwhelm, developmental stage) with age-appropriate expectations reality check
- boundary_system: object -- Clear rules, predictable consequences, and reward structures tailored to the child's age and temperament
- response_scripts: array -- Word-for-word phrases for the 5 hardest recurring moments, with calm alternatives to yelling
- de_escalation_toolkit: object -- Techniques for calming yourself and your child during meltdowns, including when to walk away
- repair_protocol: object -- How to reconnect after a blow-up, model accountability, and turn ruptures into teaching moments

## Execution

### Phase 1: ASSESS
**Entry criteria:** At least the child's age and 1-2 specific behavior challenges provided.
**Actions:**
1. Determine the child's developmental stage and what's actually age-appropriate. A 2-year-old saying "no" to everything isn't defiant -- they're doing their job. A 7-year-old lying about brushing teeth is normal boundary-testing, not a character flaw. A 14-year-old rolling their eyes is developmentally on track even if it makes your blood boil.
2. Identify the function of the behavior. Every challenging behavior serves a purpose:
   - **Attention-seeking:** Acting out because negative attention beats no attention
   - **Control/autonomy:** Pushing back because they feel powerless (extremely common ages 2-4 and 12-16)
   - **Overwhelm/dysregulation:** Melting down because their nervous system is maxed out (hunger, tiredness, sensory overload, big emotions they can't name)
   - **Skill deficit:** Can't do what's being asked because they literally don't have the brain development yet
   - **Communication:** Telling you something is wrong the only way they know how
3. Reality-check parental expectations against developmental capacity. A 4-year-old cannot sit still for an hour at a restaurant. A 10-year-old cannot consistently remember chores without reminders. A teenager cannot always regulate their emotions -- their prefrontal cortex is under construction until age 25.
4. Assess the household dynamics. If there are two parents with different discipline styles, that's the first problem to solve. If there's a new sibling, a divorce, or a move, that context changes everything.
5. Note any diagnoses or suspected neurodivergence. ADHD, autism, anxiety, and sensory processing differences require fundamentally different discipline approaches. What looks like defiance might be executive function failure or sensory overwhelm.

**Output:** Behavior assessment with root cause analysis, developmental reality check, and adjusted expectations.
**Quality gate:** Behavior is analyzed by function, not just surface appearance. Age-appropriate expectations are clearly stated. Any neurodivergent considerations are flagged.

### Phase 2: FRAMEWORK
**Entry criteria:** Behavior assessment complete.
**Actions:**
1. Establish 3-5 non-negotiable household rules (not 15 -- kids can't track more than a handful). Frame them as what TO do, not just what NOT to do:
   - Instead of "no hitting" → "we use gentle hands" (ages 2-5)
   - Instead of "stop screaming" → "use your talking voice when you need something" (ages 3-7)
   - Instead of "don't be disrespectful" → "we disagree respectfully in this house" (ages 8+)
2. Build a consequence system that's predictable, proportional, and connected to the behavior:
   - **Ages 2-4:** Redirection, brief time-ins (not time-outs -- sit WITH them), natural consequences (threw the toy, toy goes away for 10 minutes)
   - **Ages 5-8:** Loss of specific privileges connected to the behavior, earning back privileges through positive behavior, logical consequences
   - **Ages 9-12:** Collaborative problem-solving, written behavior contracts, loss of technology/privileges with clear restoration path
   - **Ages 13+:** Natural consequences wherever safe, loss of privileges with discussion, collaborative rule-setting (they follow rules better when they help make them)
3. Create a positive reinforcement system that doesn't require bribery:
   - Specific praise ("I noticed you shared with your sister without being asked" beats "good job")
   - Catch them being good more often than catching them being bad (aim for 5:1 ratio)
   - For younger kids: simple sticker charts for 1-2 specific behaviors (not everything at once)
   - For older kids: expanded privileges and independence as trust is demonstrated
4. Define what's NOT worth a battle. Pick your hills carefully. Mismatched socks? Let it go. Wants to dye their hair? Let it go. Refusing to wear a coat? Natural consequence (they'll be cold). Save your authority for safety, respect, and responsibility.
5. Align both parents (or all caregivers) on the same page. Kids will exploit inconsistency like tiny lawyers. If parents disagree, discuss privately and present a united front.

**Output:** Household rules (3-5 max), age-appropriate consequence system, positive reinforcement approach, "not worth it" list, and caregiver alignment plan.
**Quality gate:** Rules are stated positively. Consequences are connected to behaviors (not random punishment). Positive-to-negative ratio is at least 3:1. Both caregivers agree.

### Phase 3: SCRIPTS
**Entry criteria:** Framework established.
**Actions:**
1. Write word-for-word scripts for the top 5 recurring challenging moments. These should be short, calm, and repeatable even when you're exhausted:
   - **Defiance/refusal:** "I hear that you don't want to. The rule is [X]. You can choose to [do it now] or [consequence]. Which do you pick?"
   - **Tantrum/meltdown:** "You're having a really hard time right now. I'm right here. When you're ready, we'll figure this out together."
   - **Backtalk:** "I can see you're frustrated. I want to hear what you think, but I need you to say it respectfully. Try again."
   - **Sibling conflict:** "I'm not going to figure out who started it. What are you going to do to solve this? I'll help if you need ideas."
   - **Lying:** "I'm going to give you a chance to tell me what really happened. The consequence for the thing you did is [X]. The consequence for lying about it is [X + more]. Your choice."
2. Create "instead of yelling" replacement phrases:
   - Instead of "HOW MANY TIMES DO I HAVE TO TELL YOU" → "This is hard to remember. Let's figure out a way to help you remember."
   - Instead of "BECAUSE I SAID SO" → "The reason is [brief explanation]. I know you disagree, and the answer is still no."
   - Instead of "WHAT IS WRONG WITH YOU" → "That wasn't okay. Let's talk about what happened."
   - Instead of "I'M DONE" → "I need a minute to calm down. We'll talk about this in 5 minutes."
3. Build transition warnings for toddlers and young kids (most meltdowns happen during transitions): "In 5 minutes we're leaving the park. In 2 minutes we're leaving. Okay, one more slide, then we go."
4. Create age-appropriate explanation templates for when kids ask "why" about rules. Younger kids get short, concrete reasons. Older kids get real reasoning and can negotiate within limits.
5. Write repair scripts for after you lose your temper (because you will): "I yelled at you earlier and that wasn't okay. I was frustrated, but that's not an excuse. I'm sorry. Here's what I'm going to try to do differently next time."

**Output:** 5+ situation-specific scripts, yelling replacement phrases, transition warnings, explanation templates, and repair scripts.
**Quality gate:** Scripts are short enough to remember under stress. They acknowledge the child's feelings. They offer choices where possible. Repair scripts model accountability.

### Phase 4: DE-ESCALATE
**Entry criteria:** Scripts created.
**Actions:**
1. Build a personal regulation toolkit for the parent. You cannot regulate a dysregulated child if you are also dysregulated:
   - **The 5-second pause:** Before responding to ANY behavior, take one breath. Just one. This breaks the autopilot yelling response.
   - **The walk-away:** If you feel rage building, say "I need a minute" and physically leave the room (if the child is safe). This is not giving up. This is modeling self-regulation.
   - **The volume drop:** When you want to yell, whisper instead. Kids actually listen more carefully to whispers.
   - **The body check:** Are YOU hungry, tired, or stressed? Parent dysregulation is the #1 predictor of harsh discipline.
2. Create a child de-escalation sequence by age:
   - **Ages 2-4:** Get on their level physically, validate the feeling ("you're SO mad"), offer comfort or distraction, wait it out (tantrums have a natural arc of ~90 seconds if you don't fuel them)
   - **Ages 5-8:** Name the emotion together, offer a calm-down choice (deep breaths, squeeze a pillow, go to their room for a reset), talk about it after they're calm (NEVER during the peak)
   - **Ages 9-12:** Give space if they want it, offer to listen without fixing, use "on a scale of 1-10 how upset are you" to help them quantify and therefore manage the feeling
   - **Ages 13+:** Respect their need for space, don't demand eye contact or immediate compliance, come back to it later, text if talking face-to-face is too heated
3. Identify the household's meltdown triggers and build prevention strategies: after-school crash (have snacks ready), morning rush (prep the night before), bedtime battles (consistent routine starting 30 minutes earlier than you think).
4. Set up an emergency plan for when everything falls apart: who tags in, where the child can go safely, what "good enough" parenting looks like on the worst days (everyone alive and fed = success).
5. Track patterns over 2 weeks. Log when meltdowns happen, what preceded them, and how they ended. Patterns reveal the real problem faster than any parenting book.

**Output:** Parent self-regulation toolkit, age-specific child de-escalation steps, trigger prevention strategies, emergency plan, and behavior tracking template.
**Quality gate:** Parent regulation comes BEFORE child regulation in every scenario. Strategies are realistic for stressed, tired parents. Emergency plan acknowledges bad days happen.

### Phase 5: REPAIR
**Entry criteria:** De-escalation toolkit built.
**Actions:**
1. Establish the repair habit. Every time discipline goes sideways (you yelled, overreacted, gave an unfair consequence), circle back and own it:
   - Wait until everyone is calm (usually 15-30 minutes minimum)
   - Name what you did wrong specifically: "I raised my voice and that scared you"
   - Explain what was going on for you: "I was really frustrated because..."
   - Apologize without excuses: "I'm sorry. You didn't deserve to be yelled at."
   - State what you'll try next time: "Next time I feel that frustrated, I'm going to step out for a minute first."
2. Teach the child the same repair process. When THEY mess up, walk them through it: What happened → How did it affect the other person → What will you do differently → How do you make it right. This is the actual life skill discipline is supposed to teach.
3. Build a "relationship bank account" -- you need enough positive deposits (play, laughter, one-on-one time, genuine interest in their world) that the discipline withdrawals don't bankrupt the relationship. Minimum 15 minutes of undivided, child-led time per day.
4. Create a weekly family check-in (5-10 minutes):
   - What went well this week?
   - What was hard?
   - What do we want to do differently?
   - This gives kids a voice and reduces power struggles because they feel heard.
5. Set realistic progress markers. Behavior change in kids takes 3-6 weeks of consistent response. If you change your approach and see no improvement in 6 weeks, reassess the root cause -- don't just escalate the consequences.

**Output:** Repair conversation framework, child accountability process, relationship investment plan, weekly family check-in template, and progress timeline.
**Quality gate:** Repair is modeled by the parent first. Relationship deposits outweigh discipline withdrawals. Progress expectations are realistic (weeks, not days).

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) root cause of challenging behaviors is identified, (2) 3-5 clear household rules with connected consequences are established, (3) word-for-word scripts for the hardest moments are written, (4) parent and child de-escalation strategies are in place, (5) repair and reconnection process is defined and practiced.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| ASSESS | Parent describes behavior that suggests trauma, abuse, or serious mental health issue | Escalate -- recommend professional assessment (pediatric therapist, school counselor) before implementing any discipline changes |
| ASSESS | Parent expectations are far beyond developmental capability | Adjust -- provide developmental education first, then recalibrate expectations before building framework |
| FRAMEWORK | Co-parents fundamentally disagree on discipline approach | Adjust -- focus on 2-3 rules both agree on, recommend family therapy for deeper alignment |
| FRAMEWORK | Child has diagnosis that makes standard consequences ineffective | Adjust -- switch to neurodivergent-specific strategies (visual supports, sensory regulation, shorter consequence windows) |
| SCRIPTS | Parent cannot stay calm enough to use scripts | Adjust -- prioritize Phase 4 (de-escalation) first, scripts come after the parent's regulation improves |
| DE-ESCALATE | Meltdowns are increasing despite consistent approach | Flag -- may indicate an undiagnosed issue, major stressor the child hasn't disclosed, or a consequence system that's too harsh |
| REPAIR | Child refuses to engage in repair conversations | Adjust -- keep it brief, don't force it, model repair anyway ("I'm sorry about earlier"), they're absorbing it even when they seem not to care |

## State Persistence
- Child behavior log (triggers, frequency, intensity, what worked)
- Consequence history (what was used, how the child responded, effectiveness over time)
- Parent regulation progress (yelling frequency, pause usage, walk-away count)
- Repair conversation log (what was discussed, how the child responded)
- Weekly check-in notes (patterns in what's going well vs. what's hard)
- Developmental stage updates (adjust as child ages -- what works at 6 won't work at 8)
