# Kitchen Technique Master

Teaches you the actual cooking techniques that make the difference between food that's fine and food that makes people ask for the recipe. Covers knife skills that save you time and fingers, sauce making that turns simple ingredients into something special, temperature management that's the secret behind every great steak and loaf of bread, and plating that makes home dinner feel like an event. Not a recipe collection -- this is the skill underneath every recipe. Once you have these techniques, you can cook anything because you understand why things work, not just what to do.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: ASSESSMENT   --> Evaluate current skills and identify the gaps with the highest impact
PHASE 2: KNIFE WORK   --> Build speed, safety, and precision with fundamental cuts
PHASE 3: HEAT         --> Master temperature control across all cooking methods
PHASE 4: SAUCES       --> Learn the mother sauces and their infinite variations
PHASE 5: PLATING      --> Present food that looks as good as it tastes for home entertaining
```

## Inputs
- cooking_level: object -- How often you cook, what you typically make, biggest frustrations in the kitchen, dishes you've tried and failed at, what "cooking well" means to you
- equipment: object -- What you have: knife type and quality, stovetop (gas or electric or induction), oven, thermometer (instant-read?), pans (nonstick, stainless, cast iron), any specialty tools
- goals: object -- What you want to be able to do: cook for dates, host dinner parties, meal prep efficiently, explore a cuisine, just stop burning things, impress specific people
- dietary_context: object -- (Optional) Any dietary restrictions, preferred cuisines, ingredient access or budget constraints, number of people you typically cook for
- time_commitment: object -- (Optional) How much time you can spend practicing per week, whether you prefer structured drills or learning through cooking meals

## Outputs
- skills_roadmap: object -- Prioritized technique list with current level and target level for each
- knife_skills_guide: object -- Cuts, grips, techniques, practice drills, and safety rules
- heat_management_guide: object -- Temperature principles, cooking method guides, and doneness indicators
- sauce_repertoire: object -- Base sauces with ratios, variations, and troubleshooting
- plating_system: object -- Composition rules, garnishing techniques, and plate selection guidance

## Execution

### Phase 1: ASSESSMENT
**Entry criteria:** Person wants to improve their cooking and can identify at least one area of frustration.
**Actions:**
1. Map current kitchen skills across the core competencies:
   - **Knife work:** Can you dice an onion in under 2 minutes? Are your cuts roughly uniform? Do you feel safe and in control? If you're slow, inconsistent, or nervous with a knife, everything in the kitchen takes longer and turns out worse.
   - **Heat control:** Can you sear without burning? Do you know the difference between sauteing, pan-frying, and stir-frying? Can you tell when oil is ready without a thermometer? Most home cooking problems are heat problems.
   - **Seasoning:** Do you season throughout cooking or just at the end? Do you taste as you go? Can you identify when something needs acid, salt, fat, or heat?
   - **Timing:** Can you get multiple dishes ready at the same time? This is coordination, not talent, and it's learnable.
2. Identify the "multiplier skills" -- the 3-4 techniques that would have the biggest impact on their cooking right now:
   - If food is bland: seasoning and sauce skills
   - If food is burnt or raw: heat management
   - If cooking takes forever: knife skills and mise en place
   - If food looks sad on the plate: plating basics
   - If cooking stresses them out: mise en place and timing
3. Assess equipment gaps. You don't need a Viking range and a $300 knife. But you do need:
   - One sharp chef's knife (8-inch, any brand over $30). A dull knife is more dangerous and less useful than a cheap sharp one.
   - One instant-read thermometer ($15 -- stops the guessing that causes overcooking)
   - One heavy pan (cast iron or stainless steel -- nonstick pans can't sear properly)
   - Cutting board that doesn't slide (put a damp towel under it)
4. Establish the learning approach: structured drills (practice dicing 5 onions) or integrated learning (cook meals that require the target technique). Most people stick with the second approach, but the first builds skill faster.
5. Set specific, measurable goals: "Dice an onion in 90 seconds with uniform cuts," "Make a pan sauce from fond in under 5 minutes," "Plate a dinner that looks intentional, not accidental."

**Output:** Skills map with current vs. target levels, multiplier skill priorities, equipment gap list, learning approach, and measurable goals.
**Quality gate:** Skill gaps are specific (not "get better at cooking"). Equipment recommendations are minimal and practical. Goals are measurable. Learning approach matches the person's style and schedule.

### Phase 2: KNIFE WORK
**Entry criteria:** Assessment complete, knife skills identified as a priority (or working through all phases sequentially).
**Actions:**
1. Establish the grip and stance:
   - **The pinch grip:** Pinch the blade where it meets the handle between thumb and index finger. Wrap remaining fingers around the handle. This is the only correct grip for a chef's knife. The "hammer grip" (wrapping all fingers around the handle) gives you less control and tires your hand faster.
   - **The claw:** Curl the fingertips of your non-knife hand inward, tucking them under so your knuckles guide the blade. Fingertips never extend past the knuckles. This is how you keep your fingers.
   - **Stance:** Square to the cutting board, not angled. Board at hip height (elbow at 90 degrees when hand is on the board). Comfortable shoes if you're going to be there a while.
2. Master the fundamental cuts in this order (each builds on the previous):
   - **Slice:** Smooth forward-and-down motion. The knife rocks on its curve, it doesn't chop straight down. Practice on zucchini -- it's forgiving and gives clear visual feedback on uniformity.
   - **Dice:** Horizontal cuts, vertical cuts, then cross-cuts to create cubes. Practice on onions. A properly diced onion (brunoise: 1/8 inch, small dice: 1/4 inch, medium dice: 1/2 inch) is the foundation of nearly every savory dish.
   - **Julienne:** Matchstick cuts (1/8 x 1/8 x 2 inches). Practice on carrots. Tests your ability to maintain consistent thickness.
   - **Chiffonade:** Stack leaves, roll tight, slice thin ribbons. For herbs and leafy greens. The only cut where you want the blade very sharp and the motion very gentle.
   - **Mince:** Rock the knife with the tip anchored, using the curve of the blade to chop finely. For garlic, herbs, ginger. Speed comes with practice, not force.
3. Build speed through repetition, not rushing:
   - Week 1: Focus entirely on uniform cuts at slow speed. Set a timer and count pieces per minute -- but don't try to be fast yet.
   - Week 2: Slightly increase pace while maintaining uniformity. If cuts get sloppy, slow back down.
   - Week 3-4: Build toward target speed. A home cook who can dice an onion in 90 seconds is doing great. Professional speed (30 seconds) is unnecessary.
4. Knife maintenance basics:
   - Hone (straighten the edge) with a honing steel before every use -- 5 strokes per side at a 15-20 degree angle.
   - Sharpen (actually remove metal to create a new edge) every 3-6 months with a whetstone or professional sharpening service.
   - Never put a good knife in the dishwasher. Hand wash, dry immediately.
   - Store on a magnetic strip or in a blade guard, not loose in a drawer where the edge hits other things.
5. Practice drills: buy a 5-pound bag of onions each week for practice. Dice them all. Use them in soups, stocks, sauces, and omelets throughout the week. You'll go through 50 onions in the first month and your knife skills will be unrecognizable.

**Output:** Grip and stance guide, cut-by-cut technique instructions, speed-building progression, knife maintenance routine, and weekly practice drills.
**Quality gate:** Every cut technique has a practice vegetable and a uniformity standard. Speed targets are realistic for home cooks. Safety (claw grip) is emphasized at every step. Maintenance routine is included.

### Phase 3: HEAT
**Entry criteria:** Knife skills in progress or complete.
**Actions:**
1. Teach the heat spectrum and when to use each level:
   - **Low (250-300F surface):** Sweating aromatics (onions go translucent, not brown), melting butter, gentle simmering. The "patience zone" -- most home cooks use medium when they should use low.
   - **Medium (300-375F surface):** Sauteing (food moves in the pan, quick cooking), pancakes, eggs, sauces that need to reduce. The workhorse range.
   - **Medium-high (375-450F surface):** Searing, stir-frying, getting color on proteins. The pan should be hot enough that water droplets dance and evaporate instantly.
   - **High (450F+ surface):** Wok cooking, blackening, initial sear for thick steaks. Cast iron and carbon steel only -- nonstick coatings break down above 500F.
2. Master the Maillard reaction -- the single most important concept in cooking flavor:
   - It's the browning that creates flavor complexity on meat, bread, roasted vegetables, and caramelized onions.
   - Requirements: heat above 280F, relatively dry surface (pat proteins dry before searing), and time without moving the food (stop touching it -- let it brown).
   - Common failures: crowding the pan (lowers temperature, creates steam, food boils instead of browning), not preheating the pan, cooking cold food straight from the fridge.
3. Internal temperature mastery. Get an instant-read thermometer and use it until you develop intuition:
   - Chicken: 165F (carryover will take it to 170F -- pull at 160F and rest)
   - Steak rare: 120F, medium-rare: 130F, medium: 140F (pull 5F below target -- carryover cooking continues off heat)
   - Pork: 145F (the old 160F was overcooking -- USDA updated this)
   - Fish: 125-135F depending on species (salmon at 125F is silky, at 145F it's chalk)
   - Bread: 190-210F internal for a fully baked loaf
4. Understand carryover cooking: food continues cooking after leaving the heat source. Thick proteins can rise 5-10F after removal. This is why "resting" meat isn't optional -- it's finishing cooking while redistributing juices. Rest time: 5 minutes for a steak, 10-15 for a roast, 20+ for a large bird.
5. Cooking method mastery for the three methods that cover 90% of home cooking:
   - **Pan searing:** Dry the protein, season, preheat the pan until smoking, add oil with a high smoke point (avocado, grapeseed), place food away from you (safety), don't move it for 3-4 minutes, flip once. One flip only.
   - **Roasting:** Hot oven (400-425F) for most vegetables and proteins. Space food on the sheet pan (crowding = steaming). Halfway through, rotate the pan. Finish under broil for 2-3 minutes for extra color if needed.
   - **Braising:** Sear first (Maillard), then add liquid (halfway up the meat, not submerging), low oven (300-325F), covered, for hours. This is how tough cuts become tender. Time is the key ingredient.

**Output:** Heat spectrum guide with temperature ranges and uses, Maillard reaction technique, internal temperature chart, carryover cooking guide, and detailed instructions for searing, roasting, and braising.
**Quality gate:** Temperature ranges are specific. Internal temps are current (not outdated USDA). Cooking methods include common failures. Carryover cooking is addressed for every protein.

### Phase 4: SAUCES
**Entry criteria:** Heat management understood.
**Actions:**
1. Teach the pan sauce -- the fastest way to make weeknight dinner feel special:
   - Sear protein in a heavy pan. Remove it to rest.
   - Aromatics: shallot or garlic, 30 seconds in the same pan.
   - Deglaze: wine, stock, or vinegar to lift the fond (the brown bits stuck to the pan -- that's flavor gold).
   - Reduce by half.
   - Finish: cold butter swirled in (mount with butter), or cream, or just serve the reduction.
   Total time: 5 minutes. This one technique makes you 3x the cook you were before.
2. Master the five mother sauces (simplified for home cooking):
   - **Bechamel (white sauce):** Butter + flour (equal parts, cooked 2 minutes) + milk (added gradually, whisking constantly). The base for mac and cheese, lasagna, gratins, cream soups. Ratio: 2 tbsp butter, 2 tbsp flour, 2 cups milk.
   - **Veloute:** Same as bechamel but with stock instead of milk. Lighter, more savory. Base for chicken pot pie filling, gravies.
   - **Tomato sauce:** Olive oil + garlic + canned San Marzano tomatoes + basil + salt. Simmer 30-45 minutes. Finish with butter. That's it. The best tomato sauce is simple.
   - **Vinaigrette (cold emulsion):** 1 part acid (vinegar/citrus) to 3 parts oil, with mustard as an emulsifier. Whisk until creamy. Season aggressively. Works on salads, grilled vegetables, as a marinade, and as a finishing drizzle.
   - **Hollandaise:** Egg yolks + clarified butter + lemon juice, whisked over gentle heat. The trickiest one -- temperature control matters. Too hot and you get scrambled eggs. Too cold and it won't emulsify.
3. Teach sauce troubleshooting:
   - **Lumpy bechamel:** Whisk harder. If it's bad, strain through a sieve. Prevention: add liquid slowly and whisk constantly, ensure roux is fully cooked before adding liquid.
   - **Broken vinaigrette:** Add a teaspoon of mustard and re-whisk. Prevention: add oil slowly while whisking.
   - **Thin sauce:** Reduce further, or add a slurry (cornstarch + cold water). Don't add flour directly -- it clumps.
   - **Too salty:** Add acid (lemon juice, vinegar) to rebalance perception, or add a fat (butter, cream) to mellow it. Adding a potato doesn't work. Sorry.
   - **Flat/boring:** It needs acid. Almost every sauce that tastes "meh" needs a squeeze of lemon or splash of vinegar. Acid brightens flavors like nothing else.
4. Build a "weeknight sauce" repertoire -- 5 sauces that can be made in under 10 minutes and transform simple proteins and vegetables:
   - Lemon-butter (pan sauce with lemon and capers)
   - Garlic-soy glaze (soy sauce, honey, garlic, ginger, reduced)
   - Chimichurri (parsley, garlic, oregano, red wine vinegar, olive oil -- raw, no cooking)
   - Yogurt sauce (Greek yogurt, lemon, garlic, herbs, salt)
   - Quick tomato (canned tomatoes, garlic, olive oil, red pepper flakes, 15 minutes)
5. The universal seasoning principle: taste, adjust, taste again. Every sauce needs salt (enhances all flavors), acid (brightens and balances), fat (carries flavor and adds richness), and sometimes heat (chili) or sweet (honey, sugar) to round it out. Learn to identify which element is missing rather than adding random spices.

**Output:** Pan sauce technique, mother sauce recipes with ratios, troubleshooting guide, weeknight sauce repertoire, and universal seasoning principles.
**Quality gate:** Every sauce has specific ratios (not "some butter"). Troubleshooting covers the most common failures. Weeknight sauces take under 10 minutes. Seasoning principles are teach-a-person-to-fish, not follow-this-recipe.

### Phase 5: PLATING
**Entry criteria:** Cooking skills improving, ready to present food intentionally.
**Actions:**
1. Teach the three composition rules that cover 80% of good plating:
   - **Odd numbers:** Plate 3 or 5 of things, not 2 or 4. Our eyes find odd groupings more interesting. Three scallops, five asparagus spears, three sauce dots.
   - **Height:** Build up, not out. Mound rice, lean proteins against a starch, stack vegetables. Flat food on a flat plate looks cafeteria-style. Even a small height differential adds visual interest.
   - **Negative space:** Don't fill the plate. Leave at least 25% of the plate empty. The food should be the focus, and breathing room makes it look intentional rather than heaped on.
2. Color and contrast:
   - Every plate should have at least 2-3 colors. Beige-on-beige (chicken, rice, cauliflower) is technically delicious but visually dead. Add a green (herb garnish, greens), a pop of red or orange (sauce, pepper, tomato), or a contrasting element.
   - Sauce goes under or beside the protein, not smothered over it. A spoonful spread with the back of the spoon, or dots arranged on the plate, lets the protein's sear show.
3. Garnish rules:
   - Everything on the plate should be edible and relevant. Don't put a sprig of rosemary on something that doesn't have rosemary in it. Garnish should hint at what's in the dish.
   - Fresh herbs (microgreens, chiffonade basil, chopped chives), finishing oils (good olive oil drizzle), flaky salt, and citrus zest are the four most useful garnish categories.
   - The garnish goes on last, right before serving. Herbs wilt, oils run, salt dissolves. Don't plate 20 minutes early.
4. Plate selection and temperature:
   - White plates are the easiest canvas -- food looks good on white. Matte is more modern, glossy is more classic. Either works.
   - Warm plates for hot food (run them through the oven at 200F for 5 minutes or rinse with hot water). Cold plates for salads and cold dishes.
   - Size matters: a normal dinner portion on a 12-inch plate looks generous. The same portion on a 14-inch plate looks like a tasting menu. Use the plate size that matches your intent.
5. Home entertaining plating flow:
   - Prep all components before plating. Nothing should need last-minute cooking while you're arranging plates.
   - Plate one "demo plate" first -- this is your template. Then replicate for the other servings.
   - Work protein first, starch second, vegetables third, sauce fourth, garnish last. This order ensures the most important element gets the best position.
   - For family-style serving: use platters and bowls (not pots and pans on the table). Arrange food in groups, not mixed together. Fresh herbs scattered over the top of a platter takes it from "dinner" to "event."

**Output:** Composition rules with examples, color and contrast guide, garnish guidelines, plate selection advice, and step-by-step home entertaining plating flow.
**Quality gate:** Rules are simple and memorable (not restaurant-level technique). Garnish advice is practical and available at a grocery store. Plating flow works for serving 4-8 people without stress. Every tip is achievable in a home kitchen.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) skills roadmap identifies specific technique gaps with practice plans, (2) knife work covers grip, fundamental cuts, and practice drills, (3) heat management covers temperature ranges, internal temps, and key cooking methods, (4) sauce repertoire includes at least 5 go-to sauces with ratios and troubleshooting, (5) plating system has composition rules and a home entertaining flow.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| ASSESSMENT | Person says they "can't cook" and feels overwhelmed | Adjust -- start with exactly one technique (pan sauce) and one meal (seared chicken with pan sauce and roasted vegetable). Master that before moving on. Confidence comes from repetition, not variety |
| KNIFE WORK | Person is scared of sharp knives | Adjust -- this is common and rational. Start with the claw grip on soft foods (bananas, mushrooms) where the stakes are low. Build comfort before moving to hard vegetables. A sharp knife is actually safer because it requires less force |
| HEAT | Person has an electric stove with poor heat control | Adjust -- electric stoves lag (slow to heat up and slow to cool down). Use the "two burner" method: preheat one burner to high for searing, have another at low. Move the pan between burners to control temperature instead of adjusting the dial |
| SAUCES | Sauce keeps breaking or separating | Adjust -- temperature is almost always the issue. Reduce heat. For emulsions (hollandaise, vinaigrette), add the fat more slowly. For bechamel, ensure roux is properly cooked before adding liquid. Practice on small batches |
| PLATING | Food looks great but is cold by the time it's plated | Adjust -- simplify the plating. For home entertaining, family-style platters are faster than individual plates. Warm the serving dishes. Plate the hot components last |
| GENERAL | Person doesn't have time for practice drills | Adjust -- integrate learning into regular meals. Tonight's dinner is the practice. Make the pan sauce you'd normally skip. Dice the onion with attention to technique. Every meal is a training session |
