# Friendship Reset Guide

Helps you navigate the friend stuff nobody teaches you how to handle -- drifting apart from someone you used to talk to every day, realizing a friendship is one-sided, distancing from someone who's toxic but you still care about, and deciding whether to have the conversation or just quietly fade. Friendships don't come with breakup scripts, and most people just ghost and feel guilty about it forever. This gives you an actual framework for evaluating, resetting, or ending friendships with intention instead of avoidance.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: ASSESS      --> Honestly evaluate what's happening in the friendship and why it hurts
PHASE 2: DIAGNOSE    --> Determine if this is fixable, fadeable, or needs a clean break
PHASE 3: ACT         --> Execute the right approach based on the diagnosis
PHASE 4: PROCESS     --> Handle the grief, guilt, and identity shift that comes with friendship changes
```

## Inputs
- friendship_context: object -- Who this person is, how long you've been friends, how you met, what the friendship used to look like at its best
- current_state: string -- Where things are: slowly drifting, actively draining, post-conflict, one-sided, toxic but attached, or "I'm the problem and I know it"
- specific_issues: array -- What's actually wrong: they only call when they need something, they talk about you behind your back, you've outgrown each other, they're negative all the time, they don't show up for you, you dread seeing them, jealousy, competition, boundary violations
- your_feelings: object -- (Optional) How you feel about the situation: guilty, relieved, sad, angry, confused, obligated, or "I don't even know"
- complicating_factors: array -- (Optional) What makes this harder: mutual friend group, you work together, family connections, long history, they're going through something hard, shared housing, you're both in the same community

## Outputs
- friendship_audit: object -- Honest assessment of what the friendship gives and takes, with patterns identified
- diagnosis: string -- Whether this friendship needs a reset (direct conversation), a fade (gradual distance), or a break (clear ending)
- action_plan: object -- Step-by-step approach for the chosen path, with scripts and timing
- grief_processing: object -- Framework for handling the loss, guilt, and identity shift
- boundary_template: object -- New boundaries for the friendship going forward, whether it continues or not

## Execution

### Phase 1: ASSESS
**Entry criteria:** Person is struggling with a friendship and isn't sure what to do.
**Actions:**
1. Conduct the friendship audit. Rate each item honestly on a 1-5 scale:
   - **Reciprocity:** Do they show up for you the way you show up for them? Or are you always the one reaching out, listening, helping, traveling?
   - **Energy:** After spending time with them, do you feel energized or drained? Does the thought of seeing them spark joy or dread?
   - **Safety:** Can you be yourself around them? Can you share struggles without it being used against you or dismissed?
   - **Growth:** Does this friendship support the person you're becoming, or does it keep pulling you back to who you were?
   - **Trust:** Do you trust them with your real feelings, your secrets, your vulnerabilities? Or do you edit yourself around them?
   - **Enjoyment:** When's the last time you actually had FUN together? Not obligation fun. Real fun.
2. Identify the pattern, not just the incidents:
   - One cancelled plan isn't a problem. A year of cancelled plans is a pattern.
   - One selfish moment is human. A friendship where you always listen and they never ask is a pattern.
   - One mean comment might be a bad day. Consistent passive-aggressive behavior is a pattern.
   - Write down the last 5 interactions. What was the vibe? Who initiated? Who gave more energy?
3. Check your own role honestly:
   - Have YOU been a good friend lately? Sometimes when we're going through our own stuff, we pull away and then blame the other person for the distance.
   - Have you communicated what you need? Many friendships die because both people are silently resentful instead of saying "I need more from you."
   - Are your expectations realistic? Not every friend needs to be a ride-or-die. Some friends are activity friends, some are advice friends, some are fun friends. That's okay.
4. Separate the person from the moment:
   - Are they going through something hard (depression, divorce, new baby, grief) that's temporarily making them a bad friend? That's different from who they fundamentally are.
   - Did something specific happen that changed things? A betrayal, a life change, a new partner who created distance?
   - Is this a slow drift or a sudden shift? Slow drifts are usually about life stages. Sudden shifts usually have a trigger.
5. Name what you actually want:
   - "I want this friendship back the way it was" (may not be possible)
   - "I want them to change specific behaviors" (possible if communicated)
   - "I want to care less about this friendship so it doesn't hurt" (fade)
   - "I want this friendship to end" (break)
   - "I just want to understand what happened" (conversation, regardless of outcome)

**Output:** Friendship audit scorecard, pattern analysis, self-assessment, person vs. moment evaluation, and desired outcome.
**Quality gate:** Audit is based on patterns, not single incidents. Self-role is assessed honestly. Desired outcome is specific.

### Phase 2: DIAGNOSE
**Entry criteria:** Assessment complete.
**Actions:**
1. Determine the diagnosis based on the audit and context:
   - **RESET (direct conversation needed):**
     - The friendship scores 3+ on most audit items but has one or two clear problems
     - You genuinely want the friendship to continue
     - The issues are about behavior, not character
     - There's enough trust to have an honest conversation
     - You haven't tried talking about it yet (resentment without communication isn't fair)
   - **FADE (gradual distance):**
     - You've grown apart naturally and there's no villain, just diverging lives
     - The friendship isn't toxic, just expired
     - A direct conversation would cause unnecessary pain for something that will resolve itself with time
     - They're not a bad person, you just don't want to spend your limited social energy here anymore
     - You don't owe every casual friendship a breakup conversation
   - **BREAK (clear ending):**
     - The friendship is actively harming your mental health, reputation, or life
     - Trust has been fundamentally broken (betrayal, repeated lying, using secrets against you)
     - They are manipulative, abusive, or consistently cruel
     - You've tried the reset conversation and nothing changed
     - Being around them requires you to abandon your values or wellbeing
2. Consider the complicating factors:
   - **Mutual friend group:** A break or fade might mean restructuring your entire social life. Is that a price you're willing to pay? Can you maintain distance while staying in the group?
   - **Work:** You can't fully cut off a coworker. The approach shifts to "professional only" boundaries.
   - **Family ties:** If they're married to your sibling or embedded in your family, the fade is usually better than the break.
   - **History:** A 20-year friendship deserves more effort than a 2-year one. But longevity alone doesn't justify staying in something harmful.
   - **They're in crisis:** If they're in a mental health crisis, addiction, or serious life event, the timing of your break matters. You can set boundaries now and have the bigger conversation later.
3. Reality-check the "save it" impulse:
   - Are you holding on because the friendship is good, or because you're afraid of change?
   - Are you staying out of guilt ("they need me") or genuine desire?
   - Sunk cost fallacy applies to friendships too. "We've been friends for 15 years" isn't a reason to stay in a friendship that makes you miserable.
4. Talk to one trusted outside person. Not for validation -- for perspective. "Am I being reasonable? Am I seeing this clearly?" Someone who knows you AND the situation but won't just tell you what you want to hear.
5. Make the call. Write it down. "I'm going to [reset/fade/break] this friendship because [specific reason]." Having it in writing prevents you from waffling when things feel emotional.

**Output:** Diagnosis (reset, fade, or break) with justification, complicating factor assessment, sunk cost check, outside perspective recommendation, and written decision statement.
**Quality gate:** Diagnosis matches the evidence from the assessment. Complicating factors are weighed, not ignored. The decision is deliberate, not reactive.

### Phase 3: ACT
**Entry criteria:** Diagnosis and decision made.
**Actions:**
1. **If RESET (having the conversation):**
   - Timing: Ask to talk. "Hey, I've been wanting to talk about something with you. Can we get coffee this week?" Not over text. Not at a party.
   - Opening: "Our friendship matters to me, and there's something I need to bring up because it's been affecting how I feel."
   - The issue: Be specific. "When I shared [thing] and you told [person], that hurt me." Not "you're a bad friend" -- specific behavior, specific impact.
   - The ask: "What I need going forward is [specific change]." Not "I need you to be a better friend" (too vague). "I need you to keep things I share with you private" (specific and actionable).
   - Their response: Listen. They might not have known. They might get defensive. They might own it. All of those are data.
   - Follow-up: If they hear you and commit to change, give it 3 months. Real change shows up in behavior, not promises. If nothing changes in 3 months, the diagnosis shifts to fade or break.
2. **If FADE (gradual distance):**
   - Stop initiating. Don't reach out first. See what happens naturally. If they don't reach out either, the friendship was already over -- you were just the one keeping it alive.
   - When they do reach out, be warm but less available. "I'd love to but I can't this week!" You're not lying. You're choosing to spend your time elsewhere.
   - Reduce the depth of sharing. You don't need to update them on your inner life anymore. Keep it surface level and friendly.
   - Don't explain. Fades don't require a conversation. If they notice and ask, you can either have the reset conversation at that point, or say "I've just been busy -- life has been a lot lately."
   - Timeline: a fade typically takes 3-6 months. The friendship naturally settles at a new, lower level or dissolves entirely.
3. **If BREAK (clean ending):**
   - In person if possible, phone if not, text only if necessary for safety: "I've done a lot of thinking, and I need to step back from our friendship. This isn't easy and I don't want to hurt you, but it's what I need right now."
   - You can share why if you want: "I've felt [specific impact] and it's affecting my wellbeing." You don't owe a detailed explanation.
   - Be prepared for anger, bargaining, guilt trips, or attacks. This is their pain talking. Hold your boundary.
   - After the conversation: unfollow (not block, unless they're harassing you) on social media, stop engaging with mutual content, don't check their profiles.
   - If they won't accept it: "I understand you disagree. My decision stands. I wish you well." Repeat if necessary. You don't need their permission to end a friendship.
4. Handle the mutual friend situation:
   - Don't recruit allies. Don't trash talk. Don't make mutual friends choose sides.
   - If asked: "We're not as close anymore. I don't want to get into details. I hope they're doing well."
   - You may lose some mutual friends. That's a real cost. Grieve it, but don't stay in a bad friendship to keep the group together.
5. Handle the specific edge cases:
   - **They're going through a crisis:** Set internal boundaries now, plan the conversation for when they're stable. You're not required to light yourself on fire to keep someone else warm, but basic human decency means not kicking someone when they're down.
   - **You work together:** Shift to "friendly professional" mode. Cordial in meetings, no more personal sharing, lunch with other people. You don't have to explain.
   - **They're part of your family:** Establish "family event" mode -- warm, brief, surface level. You can be kind to someone you've distanced from.

**Output:** Specific action scripts for the chosen approach (reset/fade/break), mutual friend management plan, edge case handling, and timeline.
**Quality gate:** Scripts are honest but kind. Mutual friend approach protects the group. Edge cases are handled with both self-respect and basic decency.

### Phase 4: PROCESS
**Entry criteria:** Action has been taken or is in progress.
**Actions:**
1. Expect grief. Even when the friendship was bad, you're losing something real:
   - The person they USED to be (or the person you thought they were)
   - Shared history and memories
   - Your role as "their person" and vice versa
   - Future plans you imagined together
   - Part of your identity (especially for long-term friendships)
   - Let yourself be sad. This IS a breakup. Society doesn't treat it like one, but your heart does.
2. Handle the guilt:
   - "Am I being too harsh?" -- If you did the assessment honestly and the diagnosis was clear, trust your process.
   - "They're going to be devastated." -- Their feelings about the loss are valid AND your need to protect yourself is valid. Both can be true.
   - "Maybe I should give them one more chance." -- How many chances have you already given? If the answer is "too many," this IS the one more chance -- the chance for you to choose yourself.
   - "What if I'm the problem?" -- You assessed your own role in Phase 1. If you genuinely contributed to the problem, own that. But that doesn't obligate you to stay.
3. Resist the urge to go back during weak moments:
   - Loneliness hits hardest at 2 AM, on holidays, and when you see them posting happy content on social media.
   - Have a plan for these moments: call a different friend, journal, reread your assessment notes, go for a walk.
   - The first month is the hardest. By month three, the space you created will start to feel normal.
4. Reinvest the energy:
   - The emotional bandwidth you were spending on a draining friendship is now free.
   - Deepen one existing friendship that IS healthy. Reach out, make plans, show up.
   - Try one new social activity or group. The friend gap you feel is real, and it's okay to actively seek new connections.
   - Spend some of that freed-up energy on yourself. You were probably neglecting your own needs while managing the friendship.
5. Revisit in 6 months:
   - Check in with yourself. Are you relieved? Still sad? Second-guessing?
   - If the friendship was a fade, has it settled at a comfortable level?
   - If it was a break, do you still feel it was the right call? (Almost always yes, with distance.)
   - Some friendships can be resumed in a new form later, after both people have grown. Leave that door open if it feels right. But don't prop it open at the expense of your peace.

**Output:** Grief acknowledgment framework, guilt processing checklist, weak-moment plan, energy reinvestment strategy, and 6-month check-in.
**Quality gate:** Grief is treated seriously, not minimized. Guilt is examined honestly. Weak-moment plan is specific. New social investment is actionable.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) friendship is honestly assessed with audit scores and patterns identified, (2) diagnosis (reset, fade, or break) is clear and justified, (3) chosen approach has been executed with appropriate scripts and timing, (4) grief and guilt are being processed with support, (5) social energy is being reinvested in healthy connections.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| ASSESS | Can't be objective because of emotional attachment | Adjust -- write the assessment as if advising a friend in this situation. Distance from your own emotions helps clarity. Or actually ask a trusted friend to review your assessment. |
| DIAGNOSE | The friendship doesn't fit neatly into reset/fade/break | Adjust -- most tricky cases are actually "reset first, see what happens, then fade or break if it doesn't work." Start with the least destructive option. |
| ACT | They react with hostility, threats, or social sabotage after a break | Hold firm. Document if there's harassment. Inform mutual friends simply: "I stepped back from the friendship. I'd rather not discuss details." Don't engage with drama. |
| ACT | You attempted a fade but they keep pursuing with intensity | Adjust -- the fade isn't working and you now need to have a direct conversation. "I've been needing some space. I value what we had, but I need distance right now." |
| PROCESS | You feel isolated after ending multiple friendships | Flag -- if you're repeatedly ending friendships, examine whether there's a pattern in who you choose as friends, or whether your expectations are realistic. Consider therapy to explore attachment patterns. |
| PROCESS | Mutual friends pressure you to reconcile | Set a boundary: "I appreciate that you care about both of us. This is my decision and I need it to be respected." If they persist, that's data about those friendships too. |

## State Persistence
- Friendship audit scorecard (for reference during weak moments)
- Decision statement (what you decided and why)
- Action timeline (what was done, how they responded)
- Grief processing notes (feelings over time, guilt triggers, progress)
- Social reinvestment tracker (new connections pursued, existing friendships deepened)
- 6-month check-in outcome (revisit assessment, current feelings, any changes)
