# Newborn Transition Guide

Gets you ready for the reality of bringing a tiny human home -- not the Instagram version, the actual version where nobody sleeps and everything smells like spit-up. Covers the practical stuff: sleep strategies that work, feeding fundamentals (breast, bottle, or both -- no judgment), postpartum recovery for the birthing parent, and how to keep your relationship from imploding under sleep deprivation. Built for first-time parents and also for experienced parents who blocked out how hard it was the first time.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: PRE-ARRIVAL  --> Set up the essentials (not the Pinterest nursery) before baby comes
PHASE 2: SURVIVAL     --> Navigate the first 2 weeks when everything is chaos
PHASE 3: FEEDING      --> Establish a feeding approach that works for YOUR situation
PHASE 4: SLEEP        --> Build sleep habits that keep everyone (mostly) functional
PHASE 5: RECOVERY     --> Postpartum physical and mental health for the whole household
PHASE 6: RECALIBRATE  --> Adjust your relationship, identity, and expectations for the new normal
```

## Inputs
- parent_situation: object -- First-time or experienced parent, single parent or partnered, age, support system nearby (grandparents, friends, hired help), work situation and leave available
- birth_plan: object -- (Optional) Expected delivery method (vaginal, planned C-section), due date, hospital vs. birth center vs. home, any complications anticipated
- feeding_preference: string -- Breastfeeding, formula, combo, or undecided. Any known challenges (previous difficulty, breast surgery, medication conflicts)
- baby_details: object -- (Optional) Singleton or multiples, any known health conditions, premature risk factors
- concerns: array -- What's keeping you up at night (besides the obvious): finances, relationship stress, mental health history, lack of support, housing, returning to work timeline

## Outputs
- essentials_checklist: object -- What you actually need before baby arrives (the short list, not the registry bloat), organized by priority
- survival_playbook: object -- Hour-by-hour guide for the first 2 weeks covering feeds, sleep shifts, visitor management, and when to call the doctor
- feeding_plan: object -- Step-by-step feeding approach with troubleshooting for common problems, supply tracking, and when to adjust
- sleep_strategy: object -- Age-appropriate sleep guidance from newborn through 4 months, including safe sleep rules and realistic expectations
- recovery_plan: object -- Physical recovery timeline, mental health monitoring checklist, and partner support duties
- relationship_maintenance: object -- Minimum viable relationship upkeep during the hardest months, division of labor framework, and communication shortcuts

## Execution

### Phase 1: PRE-ARRIVAL
**Entry criteria:** Baby is expected (or just arrived) and parent wants to prepare or catch up.
**Actions:**
1. Build the actual essentials list. Not the 47-page registry -- the stuff you genuinely need for week one:
   - **Non-negotiable:** Car seat (installed and checked), safe sleep space (bassinet or crib, firm mattress, nothing else in it), diapers and wipes (newborn AND size 1), onesies (6-8, don't go crazy on newborn size), feeding supplies (bottles if formula/pumping, nursing pillow if breastfeeding), burp cloths (more than you think)
   - **Actually important:** White noise machine, swaddles (2-3), diaper cream, infant Tylenol (for later but have it), thermometer, nail clippers (baby nails are tiny razors), postpartum supplies for birthing parent
   - **Can wait:** Stroller (you won't leave the house for 2 weeks), fancy nursery furniture, baby monitor (they'll sleep near you initially), most clothes above 3-month size
2. Set up the postpartum station: a corner of the couch or bed with everything within arm's reach -- water bottle, snacks, phone charger, burp cloths, remote, nipple cream if breastfeeding. You will live here.
3. Prep food BEFORE baby arrives. Cook and freeze 10-15 meals. Accept every offer of food delivery. Set up grocery delivery accounts. The number one thing that falls apart postpartum is feeding the adults.
4. Have the "who does what" conversation with your partner NOW:
   - Night shift schedule (split the night or alternate nights -- never both be awake all night)
   - Who handles visitors and when (first week should be minimal visitors, no matter what grandma says)
   - Who manages which household tasks (laundry, dishes, pet care, older kids)
5. Set up a pediatrician appointment for 3-5 days after birth. Research lactation consultants now if planning to breastfeed -- you'll need one on short notice if things get hard.

**Output:** Priority-tiered essentials checklist, postpartum station setup, meal prep plan, division of labor draft, and key appointments scheduled.
**Quality gate:** Essentials are separated from nice-to-haves. Food prep is actually done (not just planned). Partner roles are assigned, not vague.

### Phase 2: SURVIVAL
**Entry criteria:** Baby is home (or about to be).
**Actions:**
1. Establish the first-week rhythm. It's not a schedule -- it's a cycle:
   - Baby eats (every 2-3 hours, including overnight, yes really)
   - Baby sleeps (in 1-3 hour chunks, unpredictably)
   - Diaper changes (8-12 per day is normal)
   - Brief awake time (newborns can only handle 45-90 minutes awake before needing to sleep again)
   - Track feeds, diapers, and sleep in an app or on paper. It prevents the 3 AM "wait, did we already feed?" panic.
2. Know the "call the doctor NOW" signs:
   - Fever over 100.4°F (38°C) in a newborn is an emergency -- go to the ER, not urgent care
   - Jaundice (yellowing skin/eyes) that's worsening after day 3
   - Fewer than 6 wet diapers in 24 hours after day 4 (dehydration risk)
   - Inconsolable crying for more than 3 hours straight (rule of 3s for colic: 3+ hours, 3+ days a week)
   - Any breathing difficulty, blue tinge around lips, or lethargy (hard to wake for feeds)
3. Manage visitors ruthlessly. Well-meaning family can derail recovery:
   - Week 1: Only people who will hold the baby while YOU nap or who bring food and do dishes
   - No one who expects to be hosted or entertained
   - No kissing the baby's face (RSV and cold sores are serious for newborns)
   - It's okay to say no. It's okay to ask people to leave. Put the partner on bouncer duty.
4. Lower every standard you have. The house will be messy. You will wear the same shirt for 3 days. Dinner might be cereal. None of this matters. The only metrics that matter: baby is fed, baby is safe, parents are surviving.
5. Sleep when the baby sleeps (yes, this advice is annoying, and yes, it's the most important thing). Even a 20-minute nap during a daytime sleep cycle makes a measurable difference in cognitive function and mood.

**Output:** Feed/sleep/diaper tracking system, emergency medical reference card, visitor management policy, lowered-standards permission slip, and nap strategy.
**Quality gate:** Emergency signs are clearly listed and accessible at 3 AM. Visitor boundaries are specific and communicated. Sleep strategy is realistic, not aspirational.

### Phase 3: FEEDING
**Entry criteria:** Baby is feeding (or struggling to).
**Actions:**
1. If breastfeeding:
   - Expect it to be hard for the first 2-3 weeks. Painful latch, cluster feeding, supply anxiety, and feeling like a human vending machine are all normal.
   - Learn the one thing that matters most: proper latch. Baby's mouth should cover most of the areola, not just the nipple. If it hurts beyond mild tenderness, the latch needs adjustment.
   - Feed on demand for the first 4-6 weeks (don't try to schedule). This establishes supply. Cluster feeding (baby wants to eat every 30-60 minutes for hours) is normal and doesn't mean you're not producing enough.
   - Signs of adequate intake: 6+ wet diapers daily after day 4, baby gaining weight at pediatrician visits, baby seems satisfied after feeds (at least sometimes).
   - Get a lactation consultant at the FIRST sign of trouble. Day 3, not day 14. Early intervention prevents most breastfeeding failures.
2. If formula feeding:
   - No guilt. Formula is nutritionally complete and millions of thriving adults were formula-fed.
   - Follow preparation instructions exactly -- water temperature, scoop measurements, no extra water to "stretch" formula.
   - Pace feeding: hold bottle horizontal, let baby take breaks, don't force them to finish. This prevents overfeeding and gas.
   - Expect to try 1-2 brands before finding one that works with baby's digestion. Spit-up is normal. Projectile vomiting is not.
3. If combo feeding (breast + formula):
   - This is completely valid and often the most sustainable long-term approach.
   - Introduce a bottle by week 3-4 if breastfeeding is established, so baby accepts both.
   - Be aware that supplementing with formula will reduce breast milk supply unless you pump during missed nursing sessions.
4. Track feeding patterns for the first month. Note times, duration (breast) or amounts (bottle), and any fussiness or spit-up. This data is gold at the pediatrician visit.
5. Address the partner's role in feeding: if breastfeeding, partner handles burping, diaper changes, and bringing water/snacks to the nursing parent. If bottle feeding, partner takes equal or majority night feeds so the recovering parent can sleep.

**Output:** Feeding approach with step-by-step guidance, trouble signs to watch for, tracking template, lactation consultant contact plan, and partner feeding role.
**Quality gate:** No judgment on feeding method. Trouble signs are specific and actionable. Partner has a defined role in feeding support regardless of method.

### Phase 4: SLEEP
**Entry criteria:** Feeding approach is established or in progress.
**Actions:**
1. Non-negotiable safe sleep rules (these are not preferences, they are life-safety):
   - Back to sleep, every sleep, every time
   - Firm, flat surface with fitted sheet and NOTHING else (no blankets, pillows, stuffed animals, bumpers, positioners)
   - Room-sharing for at least the first 6 months (baby in YOUR room in their own sleep space, not bed-sharing -- the risk data is clear)
   - No sleeping in car seats, swings, or bouncers (positional asphyxiation risk)
2. Set realistic expectations by age:
   - **Weeks 0-6:** Sleep comes in 1-3 hour chunks around the clock. There is no day/night distinction yet. You are not doing anything wrong.
   - **Weeks 6-12:** Longer stretches start emerging (3-5 hours). One longer stretch at night is the first win.
   - **Months 3-4:** Some babies start sleeping 5-8 hour stretches. Others don't. Both are normal. The "4-month sleep regression" is actually a brain development progression -- sleep will get temporarily worse.
3. Build sleep hygiene from day one (even though it won't "work" immediately, you're building associations):
   - Dark room, white noise, consistent pre-sleep routine (even at 2 weeks, a simple diaper change → swaddle → white noise → feed → drowsy placement builds associations)
   - Learn drowsy-but-awake placement. It won't work every time for months, but practicing it builds the skill over time.
   - Distinguish between sleep sounds and wake sounds. Newborns are LOUD sleepers -- grunts, squeaks, and brief cries during sleep transitions don't require intervention. Wait 30-60 seconds before responding.
4. Create a parent sleep survival plan:
   - Split the night: Parent A handles 8 PM to 2 AM, Parent B handles 2 AM to 8 AM. The off-duty parent sleeps in another room with earplugs if possible.
   - If single parent: prioritize the longest unbroken sleep stretch possible. Accept help from anyone safe for even one overnight a week.
   - Nap during at least one daytime baby sleep. Not optional.
5. Know when NOT to sleep train: not before 4 months, not during illness, not during major transitions, and not if the parent's instinct says it's wrong. Formal sleep training (if desired) starts at 4-6 months, not before.

**Output:** Safe sleep checklist, age-based sleep expectations, sleep routine framework, parent sleep shift schedule, and sleep training timeline.
**Quality gate:** Safe sleep rules are clearly non-negotiable. Expectations are brutally realistic. Parent sleep is prioritized, not treated as optional.

### Phase 5: RECOVERY
**Entry criteria:** Baby is home and postpartum recovery is underway.
**Actions:**
1. Physical recovery timeline (vaginal birth):
   - Weeks 1-2: Bleeding (lochia), perineal soreness, uterine cramping (worse during breastfeeding). Ice packs, sitz baths, stool softener, and peri bottle are your best friends.
   - Weeks 2-6: Bleeding tapers off, energy slowly returns, no heavy lifting or exercise beyond walking.
   - 6-week checkup: clearance for exercise and sex (though wanting either at 6 weeks is not a given).
   - For C-section: add incision care, no driving for 2 weeks, no lifting anything heavier than the baby, longer recovery timeline (8-12 weeks to feel somewhat normal).
2. Monitor for postpartum depression and anxiety (the most common complication of childbirth):
   - Baby blues (weepiness, mood swings, overwhelm) are normal for the first 2 weeks.
   - If symptoms persist past 2 weeks, intensify, or include: intrusive thoughts, inability to sleep even when baby sleeps, feeling disconnected from baby, rage, panic attacks, or thoughts of harm -- this is PPD/PPA and requires professional help immediately.
   - Partners can also develop postpartum depression. It's less discussed but equally real.
   - Screen using the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale at 2 weeks, 6 weeks, and 3 months.
3. Partner recovery support duties:
   - Manage the household completely for the first 2 weeks. No "helping" -- owning.
   - Monitor the recovering parent's emotional state without being patronizing. Ask "how are you really doing" and actually listen.
   - Handle nighttime diaper changes and baby delivery/return for night feeds.
   - Protect the recovering parent's sleep like it's a medical prescription (because it is).
4. Nutrition for recovery: high protein, iron-rich foods (especially after blood loss), hydration (especially if breastfeeding -- minimum 80oz water daily), and whatever hot meal can be eaten with one hand.
5. Know the emergency postpartum signs (call doctor immediately): fever over 100.4°F, heavy bleeding (soaking a pad in an hour), foul-smelling discharge, severe headache or vision changes, chest pain, calf pain or swelling.

**Output:** Physical recovery timeline with milestones, mental health screening schedule, partner duty list, nutrition basics, and postpartum emergency signs.
**Quality gate:** PPD/PPA screening is built in, not optional. Partner duties are specific, not vague. Emergency signs are clearly listed.

### Phase 6: RECALIBRATE
**Entry criteria:** Past the initial survival phase (usually 3-4 weeks in).
**Actions:**
1. Renegotiate the relationship. Sleep deprivation makes everyone terrible. Before assuming your partner doesn't care:
   - Schedule a weekly 10-minute check-in: "What do you need most this week?" and "What's the hardest part right now?"
   - Ban scorekeeping ("I got up more than you"). Replace with shift systems where duties are clearly assigned.
   - Physical intimacy returns on its own timeline. Pressure makes it worse. Non-sexual touch (hugs, hand-holding) maintains connection without pressure.
2. Address identity shifts. Both parents lose parts of their pre-baby identity:
   - The birthing parent may feel reduced to a feeding machine. Encourage any activity that reminds them they're a whole person.
   - The non-birthing parent may feel useless or excluded, especially if breastfeeding. Give them exclusive bonding duties (bath time, skin-to-skin, morning routine).
   - Missing your old life is not the same as not loving your baby. Both feelings can coexist.
3. Build a sustainable routine (not a rigid schedule):
   - By weeks 6-8, loose patterns emerge. Lean into them rather than fighting for a textbook schedule.
   - Anchor the day around 2-3 fixed points (morning wake time, afternoon walk, bedtime routine) and let the rest flex.
4. Plan the return to work (if applicable):
   - Start the childcare search early -- waitlists for good daycares can be 6+ months.
   - Practice bottle-feeding 2-3 weeks before return if breastfeeding.
   - Do a trial run: leave baby with caregiver for increasing lengths of time.
   - Have a backup care plan for sick days (daycare won't take a baby with a fever).
5. Set up the support infrastructure for the long game: pediatrician you trust, emergency contacts list, one friend you can text at 2 AM without judgment, and (if budget allows) a babysitter for occasional relief.

**Output:** Relationship check-in template, identity shift acknowledgment, daily routine anchor points, return-to-work plan, and long-term support network.
**Quality gate:** Relationship maintenance is practical and time-bound (10-minute check-ins, not "communicate better"). Identity shifts are normalized. Return-to-work plan has backup layers.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) essentials are prepared and prioritized, (2) first two weeks survival plan is in place, (3) feeding approach is established with troubleshooting guide, (4) sleep strategy with safe sleep rules and parent shift schedule is set, (5) postpartum recovery plan includes physical and mental health monitoring, (6) relationship and identity recalibration plan is defined.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| PRE-ARRIVAL | Baby arrives early, nothing is ready | Adjust -- triage to absolute essentials only (car seat, diapers, feeding supplies, safe sleep space). Everything else can be ordered from a hospital bed. |
| SURVIVAL | Parent is completely overwhelmed and non-functional | Escalate -- activate support network immediately. If no support network exists, contact postpartum doula services or postpartum support hotline (1-800-944-4773). |
| FEEDING | Breastfeeding failing despite effort | Adjust -- introduce supplemental formula without guilt. Fed baby > feeding method. Lactation consultant for continued breastfeeding support if desired. |
| FEEDING | Baby not gaining weight | Escalate -- pediatrician visit immediately. Increase feeding frequency and consider supplementation while investigating cause. |
| SLEEP | Parent is dangerously sleep-deprived (hallucinating, unable to function) | Escalate -- this is a safety issue. Recruit emergency help for overnight duty. If no help available, contact pediatrician about safe sleep alternatives. |
| RECOVERY | Signs of postpartum depression or psychosis | Escalate immediately -- postpartum psychosis is a medical emergency. Contact OB, go to ER, or call 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. Do not leave parent alone with baby until evaluated. |
| RECALIBRATE | Relationship is deteriorating despite efforts | Adjust -- recommend couples therapy with a postpartum-specialized therapist. This is preventive maintenance, not a sign of failure. |

## State Persistence
- Feeding log (method, amounts, times, issues)
- Sleep log (baby and parent sleep hours, longest stretch, night wake count)
- Diaper log (wet and dirty counts, any concerns)
- Growth tracking (weight at each pediatrician visit, percentile trends)
- Mental health screening scores (Edinburgh scale at regular intervals)
- Milestone tracking (developmental milestones by month)
- What's working log (specific strategies, routines, and products that help for this particular baby)
