# Retirement Lifestyle Designer

Helps you build a retirement that doesn't suck. Most retirement advice is about money -- this is about everything else: what you'll actually do with your time, how to stay connected to people when you're no longer seeing coworkers every day, finding purpose when your identity was tied to your job, and keeping your brain and body working. Because the biggest risk in retirement isn't running out of money -- it's running out of reasons to get out of bed. Works whether you're planning ahead or already retired and wondering "now what?"

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: IDENTITY AUDIT --> Figure out who you are without the job title
PHASE 2: TIME DESIGN    --> Structure your days so freedom doesn't become emptiness
PHASE 3: CONNECTION MAP  --> Build and maintain social ties outside of work
PHASE 4: PURPOSE ENGINE  --> Find meaningful activities that give you a reason to show up
PHASE 5: VITALITY PLAN   --> Stay physically and mentally sharp for the long haul
```

## Inputs
- retirement_status: object -- Pre-retirement (timeline), newly retired (how long), or been retired and struggling. Current or former career, how much of your identity was tied to work
- daily_life: object -- What a typical day looks like now (or will look like), current hobbies, current social activities, exercise habits, how you spend unstructured time
- social_network: object -- Partner/spouse situation, close friends (how many, how often you see them), family proximity, community involvement, whether most friends are from work
- interests: array -- Things you've always wanted to try, things you enjoyed before career took over, skills you have that could transfer to new activities, topics that make you lose track of time
- concerns: array -- (Optional) What worries you about retirement: boredom, losing relevance, relationship strain with spouse, health decline, isolation, feeling useless

## Outputs
- identity_profile: object -- Who you are beyond the job, your core values, strengths that transfer to retirement life, and identity anchors that don't depend on employment
- weekly_blueprint: object -- Structured weekly template balancing activity, rest, social time, purpose, and flexibility
- social_strategy: object -- Plan for maintaining and building relationships with specific actions and targets
- purpose_portfolio: array -- 3-5 meaningful activities matched to your strengths and interests, with getting-started steps
- vitality_plan: object -- Physical, cognitive, and emotional health maintenance plan with specific daily/weekly habits

## Execution

### Phase 1: IDENTITY AUDIT
**Entry criteria:** Person is approaching, entering, or in retirement and wants to design a fulfilling life.
**Actions:**
1. Identify what your job actually gave you beyond a paycheck. Be specific: structure (knowing what to do when you wake up), social contact (people to talk to), status (being someone), intellectual challenge (problems to solve), purpose (contributing to something), competence (being good at something). These needs don't disappear when the paycheck stops -- they just become unmet.
2. List your core strengths and skills -- not just job skills, but human skills. Were you the person who organized everything? The mentor? The problem-solver? The connector who knew everyone? The calm presence in a crisis? These strengths need new outlets, not retirement.
3. Ask the hard question: if you couldn't tell anyone what you used to do for work, how would you describe yourself? If the answer is a long pause, that's normal -- and that's exactly what this phase is for. Start listing: I'm someone who... (values learning, loves being outdoors, cares about kids, builds things, helps people figure things out).
4. Identify the "retirement fantasy" vs. reality gap. Most people imagine retirement as an endless vacation. The reality: vacations are enjoyable because they're temporary. After 2-3 months of "relaxing," most people feel restless, purposeless, or depressed. Name this pattern so it doesn't blindside you.
5. Write down 3-5 identity anchors -- roles and descriptions that are true about you regardless of employment status. Examples: "I'm a mentor," "I'm a builder," "I'm someone who keeps learning," "I'm the person who brings people together." These become the foundation for everything else.

**Output:** Identity profile with core strengths, unmet needs from work, identity anchors, and honest assessment of the retirement adjustment challenge.
**Quality gate:** At least 3 identity anchors are defined. The needs that work fulfilled are specifically named. The person can describe themselves without referencing their former job.

### Phase 2: TIME DESIGN
**Entry criteria:** Identity audit complete.
**Actions:**
1. Audit current time use. For one week, track how you actually spend your time in blocks: sleeping, eating, screen time (TV, phone, computer), social interaction, physical activity, productive activity, and "dead time" (sitting around not sure what to do). Most newly retired people are shocked by how much time goes to screens and dead time.
2. Build a weekly template -- not a rigid schedule, but a rhythm. The research is clear: too much structure feels like work, too little structure leads to depression. Aim for: 2-3 anchor activities per day (things that happen at roughly the same time), 2-3 days with social commitments, 1-2 days with purpose-driven activity, and at least one full day per week with nothing planned.
3. Protect mornings. Morning routine is the single biggest predictor of retirement satisfaction. Design a first-90-minutes routine that includes: getting dressed (not staying in pajamas -- it changes your brain), some form of movement, and one thing that engages your mind. The person who starts the day in pajamas watching news at 11am is on a bad trajectory.
4. Build in transitions. Work gave you built-in transitions (commute, meetings, lunch). Without them, days blur together and weeks vanish. Create your own: walk before "starting" your day, change locations between activities, have a specific end-of-productive-time ritual.
5. Plan the danger zones. For most retirees these are: weekday mornings (when everyone else is at work), Sunday evenings (when the "what am I doing with my life" dread hits hardest), and winter months (less outdoor activity, more isolation). Have specific plans for these windows.

**Output:** Weekly blueprint with anchor activities, protected morning routine, transition rituals, and danger zone plans.
**Quality gate:** Template has at least 2 anchor activities per day. No more than 2 consecutive days without social contact. At least one purpose-driven activity per week. Free/flexible time is intentionally included (not just leftover).

### Phase 3: CONNECTION MAP
**Entry criteria:** Weekly blueprint drafted.
**Actions:**
1. Do the relationship inventory. List everyone you see or talk to at least once a month. Categorize: close friends (deep conversations, mutual support), activity friends (golf buddy, book club), acquaintances (neighbors, store clerks you chat with), family. Count each category. Research says you need: at least 2-3 close friends, 5-10 activity friends, and regular casual social contact to avoid isolation.
2. Identify the work-dependent relationships. If most of your social contact came through work, expect 80% of those relationships to fade within 6 months of retirement. This isn't personal -- it's structural. The ones who survive are the ones you actively maintain outside the work context. Reach out now and establish non-work routines (monthly dinner, shared hobby).
3. Build new social infrastructure. You need places where people know your name and expect to see you: a gym at the same time each day, a coffee shop, a volunteer crew, a class, a club. The "third place" (not home, not work) is essential. Join at least 2 recurring group activities in the first 3 months -- even if it feels forced. It takes 50 hours of contact to go from acquaintance to friend.
4. Address the spouse/partner adjustment. If you're suddenly home all day with a partner who had their own routine, expect friction. Common issues: one person feeling smothered, disagreements about how to spend time, one partner becoming the other's entire social life (unhealthy for both). Solution: maintain separate activities and friend groups while building shared ones. Have the explicit conversation about how much together-time vs. apart-time works for both of you.
5. Combat the isolation spiral. Warning signs: going multiple days without leaving the house, turning down invitations, feeling like "it's too much effort" to see people, increased alcohol use, sleeping more. If you notice these, treat it as seriously as a physical symptom -- because social isolation has the same mortality risk as smoking 15 cigarettes a day. Take one small action: text one person, walk to the coffee shop, show up to one thing.

**Output:** Relationship inventory with gap analysis, list of 3-5 specific social activities to join, partner adjustment plan, and isolation warning signs with intervention steps.
**Quality gate:** At least 3 new social activities are identified with dates to start. Close friend count is at least 2 (or a plan to build it). Partner/spouse conversation is planned or completed. Isolation warning system is in place.

### Phase 4: PURPOSE ENGINE
**Entry criteria:** Connection map built.
**Actions:**
1. Distinguish between hobbies and purpose. Hobbies fill time (and that's fine). Purpose gives you a reason to be needed. You need both, but purpose is what prevents the "what's the point?" feeling. Purpose activities involve: using your strengths, contributing to something beyond yourself, being needed or expected by others, and seeing the impact of your effort.
2. Build a purpose portfolio -- not one big thing, but 3-5 activities across different categories:
   - **Mentoring/Teaching:** Share your career expertise with young professionals, community college students, or nonprofit organizations. SCORE (free business mentoring), Junior Achievement, tutoring programs, or simply being the person younger colleagues call for advice.
   - **Creating:** Projects with tangible outcomes -- woodworking, gardening, writing, art, cooking for others, restoration projects. Something where you can see what you made at the end of the day.
   - **Contributing:** Volunteer work that uses your actual skills (not just stuffing envelopes). A retired accountant doing free tax prep, a retired teacher tutoring, a retired manager running a nonprofit board. Nonprofits are desperate for competent help.
   - **Learning:** Take classes, audit college courses (many universities allow free auditing for seniors), join a Great Courses discussion group, learn an instrument, study a language. The brain needs novelty.
   - **Part-time work:** Not because you need money, but because some people genuinely thrive with some work structure. Consulting, seasonal work, passion projects that happen to pay. The key: it has to be on your terms.
3. For each purpose activity, define: what it is, when you'll start, the first specific step to take (not "look into volunteering" but "call the food bank Tuesday at 10am"), what strength it uses, and how you'll know if it's working for you.
4. Test and iterate. Commit to trying each activity for at least 6 weeks before deciding it's not for you. Some things feel awkward at first (especially if you were senior at work and now you're the new person). Give it time. If after 6 weeks it drains you more than it fills you, swap it out -- there's no failure in finding what fits.
5. Watch for the "overcommitment rebound." Some people go from doing nothing to signing up for everything. If your retirement schedule is busier than your work schedule and you're stressed about commitments, you've overcorrected. Cap purpose activities at 15-20 hours per week to start. You can always add more.

**Output:** Purpose portfolio with 3-5 activities, first-step action items for each, 6-week trial schedule, and overcommitment guardrails.
**Quality gate:** At least 3 purpose activities are defined. Each has a specific first step with a date. At least one activity involves other people (not all solo). Total weekly commitment is reasonable (under 20 hours to start).

### Phase 5: VITALITY PLAN
**Entry criteria:** Purpose portfolio defined.
**Actions:**
1. Build the physical activity foundation. The minimum for healthy aging: 150 minutes per week of moderate activity (brisk walking counts), 2 sessions of strength training (preventing falls is the goal -- muscle loss accelerates after 65), and daily balance work (stand on one foot while brushing teeth, tai chi, yoga). If you're not currently active, start with 10-minute walks and add 5 minutes per week. Consistency beats intensity.
2. Protect cognitive function. The evidence-based strategies: learn new skills (especially ones that are hard -- a new language, instrument, or complex game), maintain social engagement (isolation accelerates cognitive decline), exercise (yes, physical exercise is one of the best things for your brain), sleep 7-8 hours, and manage cardiovascular risk factors (what's good for your heart is good for your brain). Crossword puzzles are fine but overrated -- novelty and social learning matter more.
3. Address sleep. Retirement often destroys sleep patterns because the schedule anchor is gone. Rules: wake up at the same time every day (even weekends), no screens in bed, limit naps to 20 minutes before 2pm, keep the bedroom cool and dark, and if you can't sleep after 20 minutes get up and do something boring until you're sleepy. Poor sleep isn't a normal part of aging -- if you're not sleeping well, tell your doctor.
4. Build the mental health maintenance routine. Check in with yourself weekly: Am I looking forward to anything this week? Am I enjoying things I used to enjoy? Am I irritable or withdrawn? Am I worrying more than usual? Depression in retirement is massively underdiagnosed because people attribute symptoms to "just aging." It's not. It's treatable. If symptoms persist for more than 2 weeks, talk to your doctor. There's no shame in it.
5. Create the annual health maintenance calendar. Schedule: annual physical, dental cleaning (2x/year), eye exam, hearing test (annually after 65), recommended cancer screenings, flu and pneumonia vaccines, cognitive screening (ask your doctor at annual physical). Put these on the calendar now, not "when I get around to it."

**Output:** Weekly exercise plan, cognitive fitness activities, sleep hygiene protocol, mental health check-in template, and annual health maintenance calendar.
**Quality gate:** Exercise plan includes cardio, strength, and balance. Cognitive activities involve novelty (not just routine puzzles). Sleep routine has specific times. Mental health warning signs are listed with specific action steps. All annual appointments are scheduled or have scheduling dates set.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) identity anchors are defined beyond former career, (2) weekly template provides structure without rigidity, (3) social plan addresses relationship maintenance and new connections, (4) at least 3 purpose activities are identified with first steps, (5) physical, cognitive, and mental health plans are specific and actionable.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| IDENTITY AUDIT | Person can't separate identity from career | Adjust -- start with childhood interests and non-work accomplishments. Ask partner or friends "what would you say I'm about besides work?" Sometimes others see us more clearly |
| TIME DESIGN | Person resists any structure ("I retired to be free") | Adjust -- frame it as rhythm, not schedule. Start with just a morning routine and one anchor activity. Build gradually as they experience the benefits |
| CONNECTION MAP | Person is introverted and dreads social obligations | Adjust -- focus on smaller, deeper connections rather than group activities. One-on-one walks, small book clubs, online communities for specific interests. Quality over quantity |
| CONNECTION MAP | Spouse conflict over togetherness expectations | Escalate -- this is a relationship issue. Suggest couples counseling or at minimum a structured conversation using the "ideal week" exercise where each person maps their preferred time use |
| PURPOSE ENGINE | Nothing feels meaningful after a high-powered career | Adjust -- this is an ego adjustment, not a meaning problem. Start with small contributions and notice the impact. A retired CEO tutoring a kid in math might change that kid's life -- that matters more than quarterly earnings |
| VITALITY PLAN | Chronic health conditions limit physical activity | Adjust -- work with their doctor or physical therapist to find appropriate activities. Chair exercises, water aerobics, gentle yoga, and walking (even short distances) all count. Something is always better than nothing |
| VITALITY PLAN | Signs of depression or cognitive decline | Escalate -- this is not a lifestyle design problem anymore. Connect with their primary care physician immediately. Provide crisis resources if needed |

## State Persistence
- Identity anchors and personal strengths inventory
- Weekly template and any modifications
- Social activity log (what they joined, attendance, satisfaction rating)
- Purpose portfolio with trial status (trying, keeping, dropped, and why)
- Exercise log and physical benchmarks
- Mental health check-in history
- Annual health appointment tracking
- Satisfaction self-rating over time (monthly 1-10 scale on: meaning, connection, energy, mood)
