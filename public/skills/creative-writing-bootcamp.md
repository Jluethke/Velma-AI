# Creative Writing Bootcamp

Takes you from "I want to write" to actually writing -- with structure, not just inspiration. Covers story architecture, character building, dialogue that sounds like real humans talking, and a 30-day sprint plan that gets words on the page every day. Not a creative writing MFA in a box. This is for people who have a story idea (or want one) and need a practical system to get it written, whether it's a short story, novel draft, or personal essay. Works for complete beginners and rusty writers coming back to it.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: FOUNDATION   --> Assess writing goals, experience level, and available time
PHASE 2: ARCHITECTURE --> Build story structure, premise, and narrative framework
PHASE 3: CHARACTERS   --> Develop characters that feel like real people, not cardboard
PHASE 4: CRAFT        --> Sharpen dialogue, scene construction, and prose mechanics
PHASE 5: SPRINT       --> Design a personalized 30-day writing plan with daily targets
PHASE 6: FEEDBACK     --> Set up revision strategy and self-editing checkpoints
```

## Inputs
- writing_goal: object -- What you want to write (novel, short story, memoir, essays, poetry, screenwriting), target length, and any deadline (real or self-imposed)
- experience_level: string -- Where you are now: never written fiction, wrote years ago, write occasionally, write regularly but feel stuck
- time_available: object -- Realistic daily/weekly writing time (be honest -- 20 minutes counts), best time of day, writing environment (laptop, phone, notebook)
- story_idea: object -- (Optional) Any existing premise, characters, setting, or scenes you already have in mind. Even a vague "what if" counts
- influences: array -- (Optional) Books, shows, movies, or authors you love -- helps identify your natural style and genre instincts

## Outputs
- story_blueprint: object -- Complete narrative structure with premise, beginning/middle/end framework, key turning points, and theme
- character_profiles: array -- Detailed profiles for main characters including wants, fears, contradictions, voice patterns, and relationships
- craft_toolkit: object -- Practical techniques for dialogue, scene pacing, description, and point of view with examples
- sprint_calendar: object -- 30-day writing plan with daily word targets, session focus areas, rest days, and milestone checkpoints
- revision_guide: object -- Self-editing checklist, common first-draft problems to fix, and feedback-seeking strategy

## Execution

### Phase 1: FOUNDATION
**Entry criteria:** Writer has expressed interest in writing and can commit some regular time to it.
**Actions:**
1. Identify the writing goal and format. Novel? Aim for 50,000-90,000 words for a first draft. Short story? 2,000-7,500 words. Memoir essay? 3,000-10,000. Set an explicit target length -- vague goals produce vague results.
2. Assess current experience honestly. Not to judge, but to calibrate. Someone who's never written fiction needs different entry points than someone with three abandoned novel drafts. Ask: what's the longest thing you've ever written? What stopped you last time?
3. Calculate realistic daily output. New writers: 300-500 words/day is a solid start (that's about 20-30 minutes). Experienced writers returning: 500-1,000 words/day. The number doesn't matter as long as it's sustainable for 30 days straight.
4. Identify the writing environment and tools. Some people write better on phones (seriously). Some need silence, some need coffee shops. Don't prescribe -- discover what already works or what the writer wants to try.
5. If there's an existing story idea, capture it in one sentence: "A [character] wants [goal] but [obstacle] so they must [action]." If there's no idea yet, that's fine -- Phase 2 handles idea generation.

**Output:** Writer profile with goal, format, target word count, daily capacity, schedule, and one-sentence premise (if available).
**Quality gate:** Daily word target is realistic for available time. Goal is specific (not "write more"). Writer has identified when and where they'll write.

### Phase 2: ARCHITECTURE
**Entry criteria:** Writer profile complete.
**Actions:**
1. If no story idea exists, use the "collision method": pick two unrelated things the writer finds interesting (a setting + a conflict, a character type + a strange situation, a real event + a "what if"). Combine them. Most great stories start as weird collisions, not perfect premises.
2. Build the narrative spine using a three-act framework (not because it's the only way, but because it works and prevents the saggy middle problem):
   - **Act 1 (25%):** Establish the world, introduce the protagonist in their normal life, then break that normal with an inciting incident that demands a response.
   - **Act 2 (50%):** The protagonist pursues their goal, faces escalating obstacles, hits a midpoint shift that changes the stakes, then reaches the "all is lost" moment.
   - **Act 3 (25%):** The climax where the protagonist faces the central conflict head-on, followed by resolution.
3. Identify the core conflict type: person vs. person, person vs. self, person vs. society, person vs. nature/environment. Most good stories have at least two of these layered together.
4. Define the theme -- not as a thesis statement, but as a question the story explores. "Is loyalty worth the cost?" beats "loyalty is important." Theme is what makes readers care beyond the plot.
5. Map 5-8 key scenes or turning points. Don't outline everything -- leave room for discovery. But know the major beats: opening image, inciting incident, midpoint, crisis, climax, final image.
6. For short stories: compress this to one central conflict, one turning point, and one moment of change. Short fiction is about a single, powerful shift.

**Output:** Story blueprint with premise sentence, three-act breakdown, key turning points, conflict layers, and thematic question.
**Quality gate:** The story has a clear beginning, middle, and end (even if rough). The protagonist wants something specific. There's genuine conflict, not just events happening.

### Phase 3: CHARACTERS
**Entry criteria:** Story blueprint exists.
**Actions:**
1. Build the protagonist using the "contradiction method." Real people are contradictory -- a brave person who's terrified of intimacy, a generous person who hoards one specific thing. Give the main character at least one core contradiction. This is where depth comes from.
2. Define the protagonist's want (external goal) and need (internal growth). These should be in tension. They want the promotion but need to learn they're enough without it. The gap between want and need IS the character arc.
3. Create a voice fingerprint for each major character: vocabulary level, sentence length tendency, verbal tics, what they talk about vs. avoid, how they sound when lying vs. telling the truth. You should be able to cover the dialogue tags and still know who's talking.
4. Build supporting characters as foils -- each one should challenge, mirror, or complicate the protagonist's journey. No character exists just to be nice to the hero. Even the best friend should create friction sometimes.
5. Write a "character interview" for the protagonist: 10 questions they'd answer honestly (favorite lie they tell, what they'd never admit, what they do when no one's watching, the worst thing they've done, what they'd die for). This won't all appear in the story, but it informs every scene.
6. For antagonists: give them a motivation that makes sense from their perspective. They're the hero of their own story. "Evil for evil's sake" is lazy -- even a terrible person believes they're justified.

**Output:** Character profiles with contradictions, want/need arcs, voice fingerprints, relationship dynamics, and character interviews.
**Quality gate:** Protagonist has a clear contradiction and a want/need gap. Each major character has a distinct voice. Antagonist has understandable (not necessarily sympathetic) motivation.

### Phase 4: CRAFT
**Entry criteria:** Characters developed.
**Actions:**
1. Dialogue workshop: Write dialogue that does double duty. Every exchange should accomplish at least two things: reveal character, advance plot, build tension, deliver information, or show relationship dynamics. Dialogue that only does one thing is filler. Practice: take a scene and remove every line that only serves one purpose.
2. Scene construction: Every scene needs a micro-arc. Someone enters the scene wanting something. Something resists. The scene ends with a change -- in information, power, emotion, or situation. If nothing changes, the scene is a candidate for cutting.
3. "Show don't tell" -- but practically. Don't write "she was angry." Don't write "she slammed her fist on the table, her face reddening with barely contained fury" (that's showing off, not showing). Write "she set her glass down carefully. Too carefully." Match the intensity of the showing to the moment.
4. Pacing mechanics: short sentences speed up. Long, winding sentences with embedded clauses and descriptive asides slow everything down. Action scenes: short paragraphs, punchy verbs, minimal description. Emotional scenes: longer rhythm, sensory details, internal thought. Scene transitions: use white space, don't narrate the commute.
5. Point of view discipline: pick a POV and commit. First person is intimate but limited. Third limited is flexible and forgiving. Third omniscient is hard to pull off (skip it for first projects). Whatever you choose, don't head-hop within scenes.
6. The "five senses" check: in every major scene, include at least two non-visual senses. Sound, smell, texture, taste. This is the simplest way to make writing feel immersive without purple prose.

**Output:** Craft toolkit with dialogue techniques, scene construction template, pacing guide, POV guidelines, and sensory writing checklist.
**Quality gate:** Writer can articulate the difference between functional and decorative prose. Dialogue examples pass the "cover the tags" test. Scene template shows clear micro-arc structure.

### Phase 5: SPRINT
**Entry criteria:** Story blueprint, characters, and craft toolkit ready.
**Actions:**
1. Design the 30-day calendar. Structure it in weekly blocks:
   - **Week 1 (Days 1-7):** Act 1 -- establish the world and characters. Daily target at 80% of capacity (ease in). Day 7 is a light day or rest day.
   - **Week 2 (Days 8-14):** Act 2 first half -- rising action, complications. Full daily target. Day 14 is a rest day.
   - **Week 3 (Days 15-21):** Act 2 second half through midpoint -- this is where most writers quit. Build in a "midpoint reward" (day off, treat, share a favorite passage with someone). Full daily target with one rest day.
   - **Week 4 (Days 22-30):** Act 3 and push to finish. Slightly higher daily target to account for momentum. Day 28 is the final rest day. Days 29-30 are the sprint finish.
2. Assign session focus: each day gets a primary scene or sequence to write, plus a "if you finish early" stretch goal. No day should start with "figure out what to write today" -- that's where procrastination lives.
3. Build in "permission to be bad" rules. First drafts are supposed to be rough. Set a rule: no re-reading yesterday's work before writing today's. No editing during the sprint. Forward motion only.
4. Create an accountability system: daily word count log, a streak tracker, and an "emergency protocol" for the days when writing feels impossible (write for just 10 minutes, write the worst version of the scene, skip to a scene you're excited about).
5. Set milestone celebrations: completing Act 1, hitting the midpoint, finishing the draft. Small rewards that aren't "more writing."

**Output:** 30-day sprint calendar with daily scenes, word targets, rest days, milestone markers, streak tracker, and emergency protocols.
**Quality gate:** Every day has a specific writing assignment. Total word count across 30 days matches the target. Rest days are scheduled (not earned). Emergency protocol doesn't involve skipping days.

### Phase 6: FEEDBACK
**Entry criteria:** Sprint plan is set (this phase prepares the writer for what comes after the draft).
**Actions:**
1. Design a cooling-off period: after finishing the draft, wait 1-2 weeks before revision. This creates the distance needed to see what's actually on the page vs. what's in your head.
2. Build a self-editing checklist for the first revision pass:
   - Does every scene change something?
   - Can you identify the protagonist's want and need from the text alone (not just your notes)?
   - Is the climax the hardest version of the central conflict?
   - Read dialogue aloud -- does it sound like humans or textbooks?
   - Check for "crutch words" everyone overuses: just, really, very, started to, began to, suddenly.
3. Create a revision hierarchy: fix structure first (move, cut, or add scenes), then character consistency, then prose quality, then line edits. Working in this order prevents polishing scenes you'll eventually cut.
4. Prepare a feedback-seeking strategy: identify 2-3 readers (not just people who'll be nice). Give them specific questions: "Does the middle drag?" "Do you understand why the character did X?" "Where did you stop reading and have to force yourself to continue?" Vague feedback requests get vague responses.
5. Set expectations: the first draft will need significant revision. That's normal, not failure. Most published novels go through 3-7 drafts. The sprint produces raw material -- good raw material, but raw.

**Output:** Revision strategy with cooling period, self-editing checklist, revision pass hierarchy, beta reader guide with specific questions, and realistic revision timeline.
**Quality gate:** Self-editing checklist is specific and actionable. Beta reader questions target actual story elements, not "did you like it?" Revision hierarchy prevents wasted effort.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) story blueprint with complete three-act structure exists, (2) main characters have profiles with contradictions, wants, needs, and distinct voices, (3) craft toolkit covers dialogue, pacing, POV, and scene construction, (4) 30-day sprint calendar has daily assignments and word targets, (5) revision strategy is ready for post-draft phase.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| FOUNDATION | Writer has no story idea and feels paralyzed | Adjust -- use the collision method in Phase 2. Provide 3 starter premises based on their interests. Starting is harder than continuing |
| FOUNDATION | Writer wants to write but has less than 15 minutes/day | Adjust -- switch to micro-writing: 100-200 word daily flash fiction pieces that build a connected series |
| ARCHITECTURE | Story idea is too big (epic fantasy with 12 POV characters) | Adjust -- scope down to one character's story within the larger world. Save the epic for book two |
| ARCHITECTURE | Writer resists structure ("I want to just write and see what happens") | Adjust -- use a minimal framework: just the inciting incident, midpoint, and ending. Discovery writers need guardrails, not blueprints |
| CHARACTERS | Characters feel flat or generic | Adjust -- add a specific, unusual detail from real life observation. Real people are weird. Characters should be too |
| CRAFT | Writer's prose is technically fine but emotionally flat | Adjust -- focus on stakes. If the reader doesn't know why this moment matters to the character, no amount of craft fixes it |
| SPRINT | Writer misses 3+ days in a row | Adjust -- don't restart. Recalculate remaining word count across remaining days. Compress or cut planned scenes. Finishing a shorter draft beats abandoning a longer one |
| FEEDBACK | Writer hates the draft and wants to throw it away | Adjust -- normal. Separate the draft from the story. The story might be good even if the draft is rough. List 3 scenes or moments that work, and build revision around protecting those |

## State Persistence
- Story blueprint and premise evolution (how the story concept changed during development)
- Character profiles and voice notes (ongoing reference during writing)
- Daily word count log and streak data (motivation tracking)
- Sprint progress vs. plan (which days hit target, which didn't, patterns)
- Craft technique notes (which techniques clicked, which need more practice)
- Revision notes and beta reader feedback (for post-draft phase)
