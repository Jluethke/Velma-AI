# Depression Recovery Roadmap

Builds a structured, step-by-step plan to climb out of depression by creating micro-wins, rebuilding motivation from the ground up, and setting up accountability systems that don't rely on willpower (because depression eats willpower for breakfast). This isn't "think positive" nonsense -- it's a behavioral activation approach that starts with the smallest possible actions and builds momentum. Works whether you're in a deep episode or stuck in the gray fog of functional depression. **Important: this is a self-help support tool, not therapy or medical advice. Depression is a medical condition. If you're having thoughts of self-harm or suicide, contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) immediately.**

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: BASELINE     --> Honestly assess where you are right now without judgment
PHASE 2: MICRO-WINS   --> Design the smallest possible actions that rebuild momentum
PHASE 3: STRUCTURE    --> Build a flexible daily framework that carries you on bad days
PHASE 4: CONNECTION   --> Rebuild social and support connections depression has eroded
PHASE 5: RELAPSE ARMOR --> Create early warning systems and accountability structures
```

## Inputs
- current_state: object -- Where you are right now: energy level (1-10), what daily activities you can/can't do, how long you've been feeling this way, any known triggers or causes
- functioning_level: string -- What daily life looks like: high-functioning (getting through days but empty inside), moderate (some tasks falling apart, isolating), low-functioning (struggling with basics like showering, eating, leaving bed)
- treatment: object -- (Optional) Current treatment: therapy (type, frequency), medication (what, how long, is it helping), any past treatment history
- barriers: array -- (Optional) What's getting in the way: no energy, can't concentrate, don't see the point, physical pain, financial stress, isolation, caregiving responsibilities
- what_used_to_work: array -- (Optional) Activities, hobbies, or routines that used to bring you satisfaction or energy before depression hit

## Outputs
- baseline_assessment: object -- Honest snapshot of current functioning without shame framing, with specific areas to track improvement
- micro_win_ladder: array -- 15-20 graduated actions from effortless to moderately challenging, customized to current energy level
- daily_framework: object -- Flexible daily structure with non-negotiables, optional expansions, and "bad day" minimums
- connection_plan: object -- Specific, low-barrier steps to rebuild social contact at a pace that doesn't overwhelm
- relapse_prevention: object -- Early warning signs, accountability structures, and a concrete plan for when depression tries to pull you back

## Execution

### Phase 1: BASELINE
**Entry criteria:** Person has described their current state and functioning level.
**Actions:**
1. Assess current functioning across key life domains without judgment or comparison to "normal." Domains: self-care (hygiene, eating, sleeping), daily responsibilities (work, school, household), social engagement (how often you talk to anyone), physical activity (any movement at all), and things that used to matter to you (hobbies, goals, interests). Rate each 1-10 where 1 is "not happening at all" and 10 is "fully engaged."
2. Identify the depression's specific flavor. Depression isn't one thing:
   - **Fog depression:** Can't think, can't concentrate, everything feels muted and pointless
   - **Heavy depression:** Body feels like lead, exhaustion regardless of sleep, physically slowed
   - **Agitated depression:** Restless, irritable, can't sit still but also can't do anything productive
   - **Numb depression:** Not sad exactly -- just empty, flat, nothing feels like anything
   - The type matters because different types respond to different starting actions.
3. Map what's still working. Even in the worst depression, something is still happening -- maybe you're still going to work, or still feeding the dog, or still scrolling your phone. These aren't failures; they're foundations to build on.
4. Identify the biggest energy thieves: specific situations, people, or tasks that drain whatever limited energy you have. These are temporarily off the table or need to be minimized during recovery.
5. Set a realistic recovery timeline expectation. Depression recovery isn't linear -- it's more like a stock market graph. Overall trend up, with plenty of down days mixed in. Improvement is measured in weeks, not days. If medication was recently started or changed, the standard window is 4-6 weeks for full effect.

**Output:** Functioning baseline across life domains, depression type identification, existing foundations, energy thieves, and realistic timeline.
**Quality gate:** Assessment is honest without being punishing. "Still working" items are identified as strengths. No comparison to pre-depression functioning as a failure metric.

### Phase 2: MICRO-WINS
**Entry criteria:** Baseline assessment complete.
**Actions:**
1. Build a micro-win ladder starting from the current functioning level. The bottom rungs must be almost embarrassingly easy -- that's the point. Depression lies and says you should be doing more. The ladder says: doing anything counts.
   - **Level 1 (bed-level):** Drink a glass of water. Open the blinds. Change your shirt. Brush your teeth. Eat something, anything.
   - **Level 2 (room-level):** Take a 2-minute shower. Eat a real meal. Step outside for 60 seconds. Send one text reply.
   - **Level 3 (house-level):** Do one load of laundry. Cook one simple meal. Walk around the block. Make one phone call.
   - **Level 4 (world-level):** Keep one social plan. Complete one work task. Do 15 minutes of something you used to enjoy. Exercise for 10 minutes.
2. Customize the ladder based on the person's specific barriers. If eating is fine but showering feels impossible, the ladder concentrates on hygiene steps. If they're going to work but not eating, food actions come first.
3. Assign a "win value" to each action -- not as a score to beat, but as a visible record that something happened today. Three Level 1 wins in a day is a legitimate victory when your brain is telling you you're worthless.
4. Create the "bare minimum" rule: pick the 1-2 actions per day that count as a complete win regardless of everything else. On the worst days, doing just these means the day wasn't wasted. This fights the all-or-nothing thinking that depression loves.
5. Explicitly ban the "I should be doing more" voice. Write it down: "Doing less than I used to doesn't mean I'm failing. It means I'm sick and I'm still showing up." This isn't a motivational poster -- it's a factual correction to depressive thinking.

**Output:** Personalized micro-win ladder (15-20 actions), customized to barriers, with bare minimum daily wins defined.
**Quality gate:** Bottom rungs are genuinely achievable even on the worst days. No action requires sustained concentration or social performance. Bare minimum is truly bare minimum.

### Phase 3: STRUCTURE
**Entry criteria:** Micro-win ladder built.
**Actions:**
1. Build a flexible daily framework -- not a rigid schedule. Depression makes rigid schedules feel like another failure waiting to happen. Instead, create time blocks with a "good day" version and a "bad day" version:
   - **Morning block:** Good day = full routine. Bad day = water, meds, one micro-win from Level 1.
   - **Midday block:** Good day = productive tasks, meal, movement. Bad day = eat something, change positions (bed to couch counts).
   - **Afternoon block:** Good day = social contact, hobby, errands. Bad day = one text to someone, 5 minutes outside or near a window.
   - **Evening block:** Good day = dinner, wind-down routine, plan tomorrow. Bad day = eat, set alarm, put phone on charger away from bed.
2. Anchor the day with 2-3 "bookend" habits -- tiny actions that mark the start and end of the day regardless of what happens in between. Example: alarm goes off at the same time (even if you don't get up immediately), glass of water first thing, brush teeth before bed. These create a container that holds the day together.
3. Build in behavioral activation deliberately. Depression says "I'll do things when I feel motivated." Behavioral activation says "do things and motivation sometimes follows." Schedule one activity per day from the micro-win ladder that involves mild physical movement or engagement -- not because you want to, but as an experiment to see if it shifts anything.
4. Manage the decision fatigue that depression creates. Pre-decide as much as possible: same breakfast every day (or 2-3 options), same walking route, clothes laid out the night before. Every decision costs energy you don't have.
5. Build "transition bridges" between time blocks -- a specific 2-minute action that moves you from one block to the next. Example: between morning and midday, splash water on face and change shirt. These physical resets prevent the entire day from blurring into one numb stretch.

**Output:** Flexible daily framework with good-day and bad-day versions, bookend habits, behavioral activation schedule, decision pre-sets, and transition bridges.
**Quality gate:** Bad-day version is genuinely achievable. Framework doesn't punish bad days. Bookend habits take under 5 minutes total.

### Phase 4: CONNECTION
**Entry criteria:** Daily framework established.
**Actions:**
1. Assess current social isolation honestly. Depression systematically cuts social connections: first you stop reaching out, then you stop responding, then people stop trying. Map who's still checking in, who you've ghosted, and who you've lost touch with.
2. Build a connection ladder similar to the micro-win ladder:
   - **Level 1:** Reply to one text with more than "fine" or an emoji. Like a friend's social media post.
   - **Level 2:** Send one unprompted text. Call one person (even if brief).
   - **Level 3:** Accept one social invitation. Do a low-pressure parallel activity (watching something together, walking side by side).
   - **Level 4:** Initiate one social plan. Have one honest conversation about how you're doing.
3. Identify 1-2 "safe people" -- the people who won't make you perform being okay, who can handle hearing "I'm having a hard time" without panicking or giving toxic positivity. If no one fits this description, that's valuable information for therapy.
4. Create a "social energy budget." You have limited energy. Some interactions cost more than others. Categorize contacts: low-cost (comfortable silence, no performance), medium-cost (enjoyable but draining), high-cost (requires masking or emotional labor). Fill your social calendar with low-cost interactions first.
5. Address the "I'm a burden" lie directly. Depression says that reaching out bothers people. The reality: most people feel helpless watching someone they care about withdraw and would welcome hearing from you. Script a simple text: "Hey, I've been going through a rough patch. Just wanted to say I'm thinking of you." This costs almost nothing and breaks the isolation cycle.

**Output:** Social connection ladder, safe people identification, social energy budget framework, and scripts for re-engagement.
**Quality gate:** Connection steps start lower than the person thinks they should. At least one step requires zero social performance. Burden narrative is addressed directly.

### Phase 5: RELAPSE ARMOR
**Entry criteria:** Connection plan created.
**Actions:**
1. Map your personal early warning signs -- the specific things that happen before a depressive episode deepens. Common patterns:
   - Sleep changes (sleeping more, can't sleep, both)
   - Appetite shifts (eating everything or nothing)
   - Withdrawal acceleration (stopping one more activity each week)
   - Hygiene slide (each day you skip a step)
   - Irritability or numbness increase
   Document YOUR specific sequence, because it's consistent -- depression follows the same path into your life every time.
2. Create a "check engine light" system: assign warning levels to combinations of symptoms. Example: skipping one shower = yellow (use the micro-win ladder), skipping showers + canceling plans + not eating = orange (contact a safe person + restart the bare minimum framework), all warning signs present + passive suicidal thoughts = red (contact therapist or crisis line immediately).
3. Set up external accountability that doesn't depend on your motivation:
   - A daily check-in text with one trusted person (even just a number 1-10)
   - A recurring therapy appointment (if applicable) that's harder to cancel than to attend
   - An app or journal for tracking micro-wins so you can see the trajectory
   - A scheduled weekly self-assessment (same day, same time, same questions)
4. Build a "depression lies" reference card -- a written list of the specific lies your depression tells you and the factual corrections. Examples: "Nobody cares" -> "Sarah texted me 3 days ago and I haven't replied." "I'll never get better" -> "I felt slightly better last Tuesday after walking." Pull these from real evidence in your life.
5. Write a letter to your future depressed self -- from today, when you have some clarity. Tell yourself what to do when it gets bad, what's helped before, and why giving up isn't the right call. This sounds cheesy. People who've done it say it's one of the most useful things in their toolkit.

**Output:** Personal warning sign map, check-engine-light system with escalation levels, accountability structures, depression-lies reference card, and a letter to future self.
**Quality gate:** Warning signs are specific to this person. Red-level response includes professional crisis resources. Accountability doesn't require the depressed person to initiate everything.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) honest baseline assessment documents current functioning, (2) micro-win ladder has 15-20 graduated actions starting from current ability, (3) daily framework has both good-day and bad-day versions, (4) connection plan addresses isolation with low-barrier first steps, (5) relapse prevention includes early warning signs, accountability structures, and crisis escalation protocol.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| BASELINE | Person minimizes or dismisses their depression ("it's not that bad") | Adjust -- use specific functional questions instead of feelings: "Can you do X? How often?" Feelings-based assessment doesn't work when you're numb. |
| BASELINE | Person is in active crisis or expressing suicidal ideation | Escalate immediately -- provide crisis resources (988, Crisis Text Line). This tool is not crisis intervention. |
| MICRO-WINS | Person says even Level 1 actions are too hard | Adjust -- go lower. "Open your eyes and notice 3 things in the room" is a valid starting point. Also flag for professional assessment -- this level of impairment likely needs medication support. |
| STRUCTURE | Person can't maintain any structure | Adjust -- drop to one single anchor: same wake-up alarm daily. That's it for week 1. Add one thing per week. |
| CONNECTION | Person has no one (genuinely isolated, not just feels isolated) | Adjust -- route to low-barrier community options: online support groups (DBSA, Reddit r/depression), warmlines (not crisis lines -- just someone to talk to), peer support apps. |
| CONNECTION | Person's relationships are a primary cause of the depression | Escalate -- abusive or toxic relationships require professional support, not a social connection plan. Flag for therapy. |
| RELAPSE ARMOR | Person has a pattern of repeated severe episodes | Adjust -- emphasize professional treatment optimization (medication review, therapy modality change). Self-help alone may be insufficient for recurrent major depression. |

## State Persistence
- Functioning baseline scores across domains (tracked over weeks and months to show progress)
- Micro-win completion log (which wins happen, which don't, trends over time)
- Good-day vs. bad-day ratio per week (looking for the ratio to shift over months)
- Social connection frequency and quality tracking
- Warning sign occurrences and what intervention was used
- Treatment timeline (medication changes, therapy sessions, and their perceived effects)
