# Couple Money Merger

Helps you and your partner combine finances without it becoming the thing that destroys your relationship. Covers the full spectrum: whether to merge at all, how to handle income differences, building joint goals when you have different spending instincts, fighting about money without fighting about money, and creating a system that works for two people who probably have completely different relationships with money. This isn't about one person being "right" about money. It's about building a financial partnership that both people can live with.

## Execution Pattern: Phase Pipeline

```
PHASE 1: REVEAL      --> Put all the financial cards on the table (the scary part)
PHASE 2: ALIGN       --> Find where your money values overlap and where they clash
PHASE 3: STRUCTURE   --> Choose and build the actual system for combining (or partially combining) money
PHASE 4: RESOLVE     --> Create a framework for handling money disagreements before they become fights
PHASE 5: GROW        --> Build shared financial goals and a plan to hit them together
```

## Inputs
- relationship_stage: string -- Dating seriously, engaged, newly married, married for years, or recovering from a money crisis
- income_situation: object -- Each person's income (ranges are fine), income stability (salaried, freelance, variable), and any income disparity
- current_system: object -- How money works right now: completely separate, partially merged, fully joint, or chaotic/no system
- money_personalities: object -- Each person's relationship with money: saver/spender, anxious/carefree, planner/spontaneous, generous/frugal, and how they grew up around money
- pain_points: array -- What's actually causing friction: one person spends too much, one person is too controlling, debt one person brought in, secret spending, income disparity resentment, no savings, different priorities, or "we just never talk about it"
- financial_details: object -- (Optional) Combined income, major debts, savings, investments, property, and any prenup or financial agreements

## Outputs
- financial_transparency_report: object -- Full picture of both people's financial situations, side by side, with no secrets
- values_alignment_map: object -- Where you agree on money, where you differ, and what the differences actually mean for daily life
- merge_system: object -- The specific financial structure (accounts, splits, automation) tailored to your relationship and incomes
- money_fight_protocol: object -- Rules of engagement for financial disagreements, spending thresholds, and veto power framework
- shared_goals_plan: object -- Joint financial goals with specific dollar targets, timelines, and contribution plans

## Execution

### Phase 1: REVEAL
**Entry criteria:** Two people in a committed relationship who need to get on the same page about money.
**Actions:**
1. Do the full financial disclosure. This is the money equivalent of getting naked -- uncomfortable but necessary:
   - Each person lists: income (actual, after taxes), all debts (student loans, credit cards, car loans, personal loans, money owed to family), savings and investments, credit score (pull it -- don't guess), monthly recurring expenses, and any financial obligations (child support, family support, etc.)
   - Include the embarrassing stuff: the credit card you maxed out in college, the savings account that's empty, the impulse purchases you hide in the trunk.
   - If one person makes significantly more, state it without apology. If one person has significantly more debt, state it without shame. These are facts, not character judgments.
2. Share your money history. How you grew up with money shapes how you handle it now:
   - Did your parents argue about money? You might avoid money conversations entirely.
   - Did your family go through financial hardship? You might hoard money or spend it immediately because saving felt pointless.
   - Were you given everything? You might not understand the anxiety your partner feels.
   - Was money used as control in your family? You might resist joint accounts because it feels like losing autonomy.
   - This conversation isn't therapy, but understanding WHY your partner handles money the way they do prevents you from thinking it's just irresponsibility or rigidity.
3. Identify hidden financial behaviors:
   - Secret accounts or stashed cash
   - Purchases hidden from the other person
   - Debt the other person doesn't know about
   - Financial promises to family members
   - Gambling, compulsive shopping, or other money-related behavioral issues
   - The rule: total amnesty for anything disclosed NOW. It's the hiding going forward that's the problem.
4. Calculate the actual combined financial picture:
   - Total combined income
   - Total combined debt (with interest rates)
   - Total combined savings and assets
   - Combined monthly nut (what it costs to keep the lights on)
   - Combined monthly surplus or deficit
5. Rate each person's financial anxiety on a scale of 1-10. This matters. If one person is a 2 (money is whatever) and the other is a 9 (checks the bank account daily and panics), the system needs to accommodate both. The 9 isn't wrong. The 2 isn't wrong. They just need different things.

**Output:** Side-by-side financial disclosure, money history narratives, hidden behavior amnesty, combined financial snapshot, and anxiety rating comparison.
**Quality gate:** Both people have disclosed fully. Money histories are shared with empathy. Combined picture is mathematically accurate. Anxiety levels are acknowledged, not dismissed.

### Phase 2: ALIGN
**Entry criteria:** Full financial disclosure complete.
**Actions:**
1. Map your money values by having each person independently answer these questions, then compare:
   - "What does financial security mean to you?" (A number in savings? A paid-off house? A stable job? Six months of runway?)
   - "What's worth spending money on even when money is tight?" (Travel? Good food? Kids' activities? A nice car? Your hobby?)
   - "What feels like a waste of money?" (Expensive clothes? Dining out? Subscriptions? Gifts? Home decor?)
   - "How much savings do you need to sleep at night?" (This number varies wildly between people and both numbers are valid.)
   - "What scares you most about money?" (Running out, being controlled by it, fighting about it, never having enough for fun, leaving nothing for kids.)
2. Find the overlapping values (these are your foundation):
   - You might both value security but define it differently -- that's workable.
   - You might both agree that travel is important -- great, now you just need a travel fund.
   - You might both hate debt -- perfect alignment to prioritize payoff.
   - These shared values become the basis for your financial goals.
3. Name the conflicts honestly:
   - "You think spending $200 on a dinner is celebrating life. I think it's irresponsible." Both are valid perspectives based on different money histories.
   - "You want to save aggressively for retirement. I want to enjoy our money now while we're young." Neither is wrong.
   - "You grew up poor and every dollar feels precious. I grew up comfortable and money feels abundant." This gap isn't about math -- it's about nervous system regulation.
   - Don't try to resolve these conflicts yet. Just name them accurately. Resolution comes in Phase 4.
4. Discuss income disparity openly (if applicable):
   - Does the higher earner feel they should have more say? Does the lower earner feel guilty or resentful?
   - Are contributions valued equally regardless of income? (Household labor, childcare, and emotional labor are economic contributions even though they don't generate a paycheck.)
   - How will this change over time? Career shifts, parental leave, education, layoffs -- income disparity is rarely permanent.
5. Agree on one foundational principle before going further: "We are a financial team. My debt is our debt. My income is our income. We solve money problems together, not against each other." If you can't agree on this, the system doesn't matter -- you need couples counseling first.

**Output:** Individual money values comparison, shared values foundation, named conflicts, income disparity discussion outcomes, and foundational partnership principle.
**Quality gate:** Values are compared without judgment. Conflicts are named without attempting resolution. Income disparity is discussed with equity, not just equality. Foundational principle is agreed upon.

### Phase 3: STRUCTURE
**Entry criteria:** Values alignment complete.
**Actions:**
1. Choose your financial structure. There are three main options, and none is universally "right":
   - **Option A -- Full Merge:** One joint checking, one joint savings. Everything goes in, everything comes out. Best for: couples with similar incomes and spending habits, high trust, and low control issues. Risks: one person feeling monitored, spending guilt.
   - **Option B -- Yours/Mine/Ours (the most common and usually best):** Joint account for shared expenses (rent, groceries, utilities, savings goals), individual accounts for personal spending. Each person gets a "no questions asked" personal budget. Best for: most couples, especially with income disparity or different spending styles. Risks: "your" and "my" categories become weaponized.
   - **Option C -- Mostly Separate:** Each person handles their own finances, split shared bills by percentage of income or 50/50. Best for: newer relationships, couples who tried merging and hated it, second marriages with complex financial histories. Risks: can feel like roommates, hard to build shared goals.
2. If choosing Option B (recommended for most), set up the specifics:
   - Joint account contribution: proportional to income (not 50/50 unless incomes are similar). If one person earns 60% of the income, they contribute 60% of the joint expenses.
   - Personal spending budget: equal for both people regardless of income. This prevents the higher earner from having all the fun money. Amount depends on income: minimum enough that both people can buy lunch, a gift, or a hobby item without asking permission.
   - Joint savings contribution: agreed-upon amount or percentage, auto-transferred from each person's pay.
3. Automate everything possible:
   - Direct deposit splits into joint and personal accounts
   - Auto-transfers to savings on payday
   - Auto-pay for all fixed bills from the joint account
   - The fewer financial decisions that need to be made manually, the fewer opportunities for conflict.
4. Set spending thresholds:
   - Under $[amount]: spend freely from your personal account, no conversation needed
   - $[amount] to $[amount]: mention it to your partner (not ask permission -- mention)
   - Over $[amount]: discuss and agree before purchasing
   - These thresholds depend on your income. For a couple earning $80K combined, it might be $50/$200/$500. For $200K combined, it might be $100/$500/$1,000. There's no universal number.
5. Handle the edge cases:
   - Gifts for each other: personal account (the joint account isn't romantic)
   - Family financial support (helping parents, siblings): must be discussed and agreed upon. This is a joint decision even if it's "your" family.
   - Windfalls (bonus, inheritance, tax refund): decide the split in advance. Suggestion: 50% to shared goals, 25% each to personal.
   - One person's individual debt from before the relationship: decide together whether to tackle it jointly or individually, and what "support" looks like from the other partner.

**Output:** Chosen financial structure with rationale, account setup details, automation plan, spending threshold agreement, and edge case protocols.
**Quality gate:** Structure matches the couple's specific situation, not a template. Automation minimizes manual decisions. Spending thresholds are explicit and agreed upon. Edge cases are addressed before they come up.

### Phase 4: RESOLVE
**Entry criteria:** Financial structure is built.
**Actions:**
1. Create rules of engagement for money disagreements:
   - Money fights happen at the table with statements open, not in bed at midnight. Schedule them if needed: "money date" every other week or monthly.
   - No scorekeeping: "I earn more" or "I paid for the last three dinners" is banned. You're a team.
   - No name-calling: "irresponsible," "controlling," "cheap," "reckless" -- these words end productive conversation immediately.
   - Stay on the specific issue. "You bought a $300 jacket" is the issue. "You ALWAYS waste money" is an attack.
   - If it gets heated, take a 20-minute break (set a timer) and come back. Walking out without a return time is stonewalling.
2. Build a monthly money date (15-20 minutes, not painful):
   - Review last month's spending together (no judgment, just data)
   - Check savings goal progress
   - Discuss any upcoming expenses
   - Each person shares one financial "win" and one "I wish I hadn't"
   - Make it bearable: do it over takeout, wine, or dessert. Associate money talk with something pleasant.
3. Handle specific recurring fights:
   - **"You spend too much on [X]":** → Is it coming from their personal budget? Then it's not your business. If it's from the joint account, it's a budget issue to solve together, not a character flaw to attack.
   - **"We never have fun / we're too cheap":** → Build a "fun fund" that's pre-approved. Spending from the fun fund is guilt-free by design.
   - **"You're hiding purchases":** → This is a trust issue, not a money issue. If it's happening, the system needs more transparency (shared apps, spending alerts) and possibly couples therapy.
   - **Income resentment:** → Reframe contributions. If one person works less but handles childcare, household management, or emotional labor, that's a contribution worth tens of thousands a year.
4. Establish the veto system:
   - Each person gets a certain number of "vetos" per year for large discretionary purchases. A veto isn't permanent -- it's "not right now" and triggers a conversation about why.
   - Both people get veto power regardless of income. The higher earner doesn't get more votes.
   - A veto must come with a reason, not just "no." "I don't think we can afford that this quarter because of [reason]" is valid. "Because I said so" is not.
5. Know when money fights aren't about money:
   - If you fight about money every week, the issue might be power, control, respect, or trust -- not dollars.
   - If one person feels they can't spend ANY money without guilt, the issue is autonomy, not budgeting.
   - If financial discussions always end in tears, the issue might be anxiety or trauma, not the spreadsheet.
   - Recommendation: if money fights persist despite a good system, couples therapy with a financial focus is not overkill. It's maintenance.

**Output:** Money fight rules of engagement, monthly money date format, recurring fight resolution scripts, veto system, and "it's not about money" diagnostic.
**Quality gate:** Rules are specific and enforceable. Money date is short and associated with something positive. Recurring fights have specific solutions. Deeper issues are flagged for professional help.

### Phase 5: GROW
**Entry criteria:** Financial structure and conflict resolution framework are in place.
**Actions:**
1. Set shared financial goals using the "Horizon" method:
   - **Short-term (0-12 months):** Emergency fund target (3-6 months expenses), pay off specific debt, save for a vacation, build the buffer
   - **Medium-term (1-5 years):** House down payment, wedding fund, car replacement, career change fund, baby fund
   - **Long-term (5+ years):** Retirement contributions, college fund, financial independence target, dream purchase
   - Each person picks their top priority at each horizon. Discuss and merge. If they conflict, compromise based on urgency and impact.
2. Build the savings plan with specific numbers:
   - What's the dollar target for each goal?
   - What monthly contribution gets you there on time?
   - Which goals run in parallel and which are sequential?
   - Automate contributions to each goal. Separate savings accounts for separate goals (most banks allow unlimited savings buckets now).
3. Tackle debt as a team:
   - List all debt by interest rate. Attack highest interest first (avalanche) or smallest balance first (snowball) -- decide together based on what motivates you.
   - Both people contribute to debt payoff even if it's "not their debt." You're a team. Their financial freedom IS your financial freedom.
   - Celebrate payoff milestones. Paid off a credit card? Dinner out (within budget). Paid off student loans? Real celebration. Progress needs to be visible and felt.
4. Plan for the big life transitions together:
   - Having kids: childcare costs ($1,000-2,500/month), reduced income during leave, increased expenses across the board. Plan this BEFORE pregnancy, not after.
   - Career change: one person's income may drop temporarily. Build a buffer first. Agree on a timeline and financial boundaries.
   - Home purchase: down payment timeline, what you can actually afford (not what the bank says you can afford -- that's always more than is comfortable), and how homeownership will change monthly costs.
   - Retirement: are you on the same page about when? Different retire-early dreams need to be reconciled. Max employer matches first (free money), then build from there.
5. Schedule a quarterly "State of the Union" financial review:
   - Net worth update (assets minus debts)
   - Goal progress for each horizon
   - System check: is the structure still working, or does it need adjustment?
   - Individual satisfaction: does each person feel the system is fair?
   - Celebrate wins: total debt paid off this quarter, savings milestones hit, investments grown
   - This review should feel like checking the scoreboard together, not an audit.

**Output:** Shared goals by horizon, savings plan with automation, debt payoff strategy, life transition financial plans, and quarterly review template.
**Quality gate:** Goals are specific with dollar amounts and timelines. Both people's priorities are represented. Debt strategy is unified. Life transitions are planned before they happen.

## Exit Criteria
Done when: (1) full financial disclosure is complete and accepted, (2) money values are aligned or conflicts are named and accepted, (3) a specific financial structure (accounts, splits, thresholds) is built and automated, (4) money disagreement rules and regular check-ins are established, (5) shared financial goals with specific plans are set at all time horizons.

## Error Handling
| Phase | Failure Mode | Response |
|---|---|---|
| REVEAL | One partner refuses to disclose finances | Escalate -- this is a relationship trust issue. Financial secrecy in a committed partnership is a red flag. Couples counseling before financial planning. |
| REVEAL | Discovery of hidden debt or secret accounts | Adjust -- process the betrayal separately from the financial planning. The money problem is solvable. The trust problem is what needs immediate attention. |
| ALIGN | Values are fundamentally incompatible (one wants FIRE, the other wants to spend freely) | Adjust -- find the middle ground: aggressive savings with a protected "enjoy life" fund. If no middle ground exists, this is a compatibility issue, not a budgeting issue. |
| STRUCTURE | System keeps breaking down because one person won't follow it | Adjust -- the system might not fit. Revisit the structure choice. If the person won't follow ANY system, the issue is buy-in, not design. |
| RESOLVE | Money fights escalate despite rules of engagement | Escalate -- couples therapy with financial focus. Some money fights are proxies for power, control, or respect issues that a spreadsheet can't fix. |
| RESOLVE | Financial infidelity (secret spending, hidden accounts) is ongoing | Escalate -- this is equivalent to relationship infidelity in terms of trust damage. Professional intervention needed. |
| GROW | One person's financial goals require the other to sacrifice significantly | Adjust -- both people's wants matter. No one should feel they're funding someone else's dream at the expense of their own. Renegotiate goals. |

## State Persistence
- Combined financial snapshot (updated monthly: income, debt, savings, net worth)
- Account structure and automation details
- Spending threshold agreements (adjusted as income changes)
- Money date log (what was discussed, any issues, any wins)
- Goal progress tracking (each goal's target, current amount, projected completion)
- Debt payoff progress (balances over time, interest saved, celebrations logged)
- Quarterly review outcomes (system adjustments, satisfaction ratings, next-quarter priorities)
- Money fight log (what triggered it, how it was resolved, any pattern emerging)
